> [!info]- Warm-up Movement Guide (click to expand)
>
> **Thoracic Foam Roll**
> Roll mid-back only (not lower back). Place the roller perpendicular to your spine, hands behind your head. Extend back over it, exhale, let gravity open you up. Move the roller one vertebra at a time from mid-back to upper back. 1-2 min. This directly primes the thoracic extension you need for overhead positions.
>
> **Banded Pull-Aparts x15**
> Hold a light band at shoulder width, arms straight in front. Pull apart until the band touches your chest, squeezing shoulder blades together. Keep arms straight, don't shrug. This activates the mid-traps and rear delts — the muscles that stabilize the bar overhead in the snatch catch.
>
> **PVC Snatch Grip BTN Press x10**
> Hold PVC or empty bar behind your neck in a snatch-width grip. Press straight up to full lockout. This grooves the overhead position and exposes any shoulder or thoracic restriction before you load it. If you can't lock out cleanly here, you'll struggle overhead with weight.
>
> **OHS with Empty Bar x5**
> Full depth overhead squat. Active shoulders (push up into the bar), midline tight, knees tracking over toes. This is a position check — if something feels restricted, address it now before loading up.
>
> **Snatch Balance x5**
> Start with bar on back in snatch grip. Dip and drive, then aggressively punch yourself *under* the bar into a full overhead squat. This trains the speed and aggression of getting under the bar — the exact quality you need to improve your snatch efficiency.

> [!info]- Snatch Complex — Mechanism & Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Why this complex?** It forces three things in sequence that address your biggest snatch limiter — positional efficiency, not strength:
>
> **1. Hang Snatch (above knee)**
> Starting from the hang removes the first pull from the equation. You start in the power position. This teaches you *where* the bar should be when you initiate the second pull — against the hip crease, arms long, shoulders over or slightly behind the bar. By shortening the pull, you have less time to generate force, which forces you to be more efficient with your hip extension and faster getting under. It exposes timing errors immediately — if you pull early with your arms or don't finish the hip, you'll miss.
>
> **2. Snatch from Floor**
> Now you do the full lift. After doing the hang, your nervous system has a fresh "map" of where the bar needs to be at the hip. The full lift should feel like you're just adding the first pull (floor to knee) as a setup to arrive at the same position you just practiced from the hang. Think of the first pull as *positioning*, not *pulling*. Keep your back angle constant from floor to knee, then sweep the bar in toward your hips.
>
> **3. OHS**
> After catching the snatch, hold it and squat again. This builds overhead stability under fatigue and reinforces the receiving position. Your shoulders, midline, and thoracic have to stay active and extended. This is where you build the confidence to catch heavy snatches deep rather than power snatching and riding it down.
>
> **Execution tips at 73 kg (70%):**
> - This should feel moderate. Focus entirely on positions, not on moving the weight.
> - Hang snatch: start with bar at mid-thigh, shoulders over bar, weight in mid-foot. Push the floor away with your legs (don't yank with arms). When the bar reaches the hip crease, extend hard ("jump") and immediately pull yourself under.
> - Snatch from floor: slow off the floor. The bar should travel in a slight arc *toward* you, not straight up. At the knee, shins vertical, shoulders still over the bar. From knee to hip, accelerate.
> - OHS: don't just bounce it. Controlled descent, active press up into the bar at the bottom, drive up.
> - **For your left side:** pay attention to whether your left arm drifts forward or bends at the bottom of the OHS. If it does, that's the thoracic mobility gap showing up. Don't fight it with strength — just note it.

> [!info]- Tempo Back Squat — Mechanism & Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Why tempo squats?** They serve three purposes in this program:
>
> **1. Tendon adaptation for your left knee**
> The 3-second eccentric (lowering) phase creates time under tension for the patellar tendon. Tendons adapt to load, but they need controlled, sustained loading — not just heavy singles. By spending 3 seconds descending, your left patellar tendon gets a graduated loading stimulus that builds its capacity over weeks. This is why we don't jump to heavy squats in Week 1.
>
> **2. Left-right asymmetry exposure**
> Moving slowly makes imbalances impossible to hide. If your right side is doing more work, you'll feel it during a 3-second descent because you can't use momentum to compensate. Pay attention to which side feels harder, especially in the bottom position.
>
> **3. Motor pattern under fatigue**
> 6 reps at tempo means each set takes ~25-30 seconds. This builds muscular endurance in the squat pattern and teaches you to maintain position (back angle, bracing, knee tracking) when fatigued — directly transferable to catching heavy cleans after a long pull.
>
> **Execution tips:**
> - Low bar position as usual. Big breath at the top, brace core hard (360-degree pressure).
> - 3 seconds down: count "one-thousand, two-thousand, three-thousand." Don't rush. Consistent speed — don't slow at the top and speed up at the bottom.
> - 1-second pause at the bottom: dead stop. No bounce. This eliminates the stretch reflex and makes you generate force from a dead stop — directly trains clean recovery.
> - Explode up: fast concentric after the pause.
> - **For your left knee:** position feet with slight toe-out (15-25 degrees). Left knee must track *over* left toes, not inside them. If patellar pain starts, check foot position first — even a small adjustment can eliminate it.
> - 133 kg at tempo will feel heavier than 133 kg at normal speed. That's the point. RPE should be ~6-7 for sets 1-3, maybe 7-8 by set 4.

## Accessory Window (15-20 min)

- [x] Peterson step-downs: 3 x 10 each leg (bodyweight or light DB) ✅ 2026-03-30
- [x] Spanish squat iso hold: 3 x 30 sec ✅ 2026-03-30
- [x] Thoracic extension over foam roller: 3 x 10 breaths ✅ 2026-03-30

> [!info]- Accessory — Mechanism & Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Peterson Step-Downs**
> The single most targeted exercise for your left knee issue. It specifically loads the VMO (vastus medialis oblique) — the inner quad muscle responsible for patellar tracking. Your left VMO is likely weaker than your right, causing the kneecap to pull slightly laterally under load, which creates the pain you feel.
>
> *How to do it:* Stand on the edge of a box or step (15-20cm high) on your left leg. Your right foot hangs off the edge. Slowly bend your left knee to lower your right heel toward the ground — take 3 full seconds to descend. Lightly tap your right heel on the floor, then drive back up through your left leg. The key: your left knee must track straight over your toes, and you control the entire descent. No dropping, no flopping.
>
> Start with bodyweight. If it's easy (RPE < 5), hold a light dumbbell (5-10 kg) next to your chest. Do left leg first, then match reps on right.
>
> **Spanish Squat Iso Hold**
> The band behind your knees pushes your shins backward, which offloads the patellar tendon while still loading the quad. This is one of the best exercises for patellar tendinopathy rehabilitation because it lets you strengthen the quad in a knee-loaded position *without* the compressive force that causes pain. Over weeks, this builds the tendon's capacity to handle the exact positions that currently hurt.
>
> *How to do it:* Loop a heavy resistance band around a rig post at knee height. Step into it so the band sits behind both knees. Step back until there's good tension. Lean your torso back (the band holds you), and sink into a quarter-squat. Hold. You should feel your quads working hard but *no* knee pain. If you feel pain, adjust depth — go slightly higher.
>
> **Thoracic Extension Over Foam Roller**
> Your thoracic spine is stuck in relative flexion (rounded forward), which is why your front rack position suffers and your left elbow can't get high enough. This drill uses the foam roller as a fulcrum to passively mobilize each thoracic segment into extension. By breathing deeply (exhaling fully and relaxing over the roller), you're using your diaphragm to create internal space and let gravity do the work.
>
> *How to do it:* Lie face-up, foam roller across your mid-back. Hands behind head, elbows together. Exhale fully and let your upper back extend over the roller. Hold 2-3 breaths. Then move the roller up one vertebra and repeat. Work from mid-back to upper back. 3 passes = ~10 breaths total.
