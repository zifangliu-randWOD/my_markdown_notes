# Friday — Week 1: Snatch + C&J + HS Walk Skill

[[00 - Program Overview]] | [[Phase 1 - Weeks 1-4]]

> [!tip] Day Focus
> Friday is the **full lifts day** — the culmination of the week. Monday you drilled snatch positions with complexes; today you put it all together with full snatches. This is also your first clean & jerk session this week. End-of-week fatigue may be accumulating, so listen to your body. The priority is quality reps with speed, not grinding. Film your snatches — compare positions to Monday's complex work.

## Warm-up (10 min)

- [ ] Snatch grip RDL x 5
- [ ] Hang snatch high pull x 5
- [ ] Snatch balance x 5
- [ ] Clean & jerk with empty bar x 3

> [!info]- Warm-up Movement Guide (click to expand)
>
> **Snatch Grip RDL x5**
> Builds awareness of the pulling position with a snatch-width grip. Feel the hamstrings load as you hinge. Keep the bar close to your legs, shoulders over the bar, back flat. This grooves the first pull position — the exact setup you need before every snatch today.
>
> **Hang Snatch High Pull x5**
> From the hip, extend hard and pull the bar high — elbows up and out, bar to chest height. This trains the aggressiveness of the second pull without worrying about catching. Think "fast elbows, high elbows." This primes your nervous system for the explosive pulling you'll need in the full snatches.
>
> **Snatch Balance x5**
> The most important warm-up drill for speed under the bar. Start with bar on back, dip and drive, then punch yourself under into a full overhead squat. This primes the exact motor pattern you'll use in the full snatch. If you feel slow or hesitant getting under, do extra reps here before loading up.
>
> **Clean & Jerk with Empty Bar x3**
> Rehearse the full clean and jerk movement. Focus on the transition — clean, recover, reset feet, dip and jerk. This grooves the sequence before loading. Pay attention to your front rack position as you catch the clean — elbows up, thoracic extended.

> [!info]- Snatch (Full Lifts) — Mechanism & Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Why full snatches today?**
> Monday's complex broke the snatch into pieces and drilled positions. Today, you put it together as one fluid lift. The goal is to apply what you practiced — higher explosive position, patient off the floor, fast turnover. This is where the complex work pays off.
>
> **Building to a heavy single at 75% (79 kg)**
> Don't jump straight to 79 kg. Build up gradually: empty bar -> 50 kg -> 60 kg -> 70 kg -> 73 kg -> 79 kg. Each weight should feel easy before you add. The heavy single is NOT a max attempt — it's a "comfortable heavy" that reinforces positions under slightly more load. If 79 kg doesn't feel right today, stop at 73-75 kg and move on.
>
> **Working sets 5x2 at 70-75% (73-79 kg)**
> Pick a weight in this range that felt good on Monday and stay there. Two reps per set means you get a second chance to correct anything from rep 1. Rest 1:30-2:00 between sets. Don't rush — full reset between reps.
>
> **Connection to the snatch efficiency gap**
> At 55% snatch-to-squat ratio, the lever is technique, not strength. Every rep today should be about: (1) patient off the floor, (2) bar stays close, (3) fast turnover into a solid overhead catch. If you hit these three, the weight goes up over time.
>
> **Athlete-specific notes**
> Note whether the higher explosive position from Monday's complex work carries over to today's full snatches. That's the measure of whether the complex is working. On Monday at 73 kg, you noticed the explosive position shifting higher — look for that same feeling today.
>
> **Expected RPE**
> At 73-79 kg these should feel RPE 5-7 — smooth and fast. If any rep feels like RPE 8+, you're either pulling wrong or fatigued. Drop weight rather than grind.

## Secondary: Clean & Jerk

4 x (1+1) @ 70% — working weight 93 kg
- Clean 70% = 98 kg / Jerk 70% = 84 kg — compromise at 93 kg
- Focus: smooth transition from clean to jerk, reset feet between

| 2   | 93 kg  |       |       |
| 3   | 93 kg  |       |       |
| 4   | 93 kg  |       |       |

> [!info]- Clean & Jerk — Mechanism & Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Why C&J today?**
> This is the first time this week you're doing both movements together. The focus is on the transition — can you clean, recover, reset, and then jerk cleanly? Many lifters rush the jerk after a heavy clean. Practice being deliberate.
>
> **Working weight 93 kg**
> This is a compromise between 98 kg (70% of your 140 kg clean) and 84 kg (70% of your ~120 kg jerk). Since the jerk is your weaker lift (C&J target is 140+ kg, jerk ~120 kg), we use the lower working weight so the jerk isn't the limiting factor in practice. The clean at 93 kg should feel very comfortable — use that comfort to focus on execution quality.
>
> **Clean execution**
> Smooth pull to hip, fast elbows in the catch. At 93 kg this should feel comfortable. Focus on a clean front rack catch — elbows high, bar resting on the shoulders, not on the wrists. Stand up fully before thinking about the jerk.
>
> **Jerk execution**
> After the clean, stand up fully. Reset your feet to your jerk stance — they may have shifted during the clean. Take a breath, brace, then dip and drive. Vertical dip (knees track over toes, torso stays upright), fast lockout overhead. Same cues as Tuesday's push jerk work.
>
> **Connection to limiters**
> The jerk is where thoracic restriction and left knee intersect most directly. The front rack from the clean feeds directly into the jerk dip. If your elbows are low from a poor clean catch, the jerk starts compromised. Focus on recovering to a strong front rack position before initiating the jerk. Watch for the dip — if your left knee drifts inward during the jerk dip, widen your stance slightly or cue "knees out."
>
> **Expected RPE**
> At 93 kg: RPE 5-6 for the clean, RPE 6-7 for the jerk. If the jerk feels harder than the clean, that's expected — the jerk is your technical limiter. Don't let frustration with the jerk make you rush. Every rep is practice.

## Gymnastics Skill: Handstand Walk Progressions (5-10 min)

- [ ] Wall-facing handstand hold: 3 x 30 sec
- [ ] Wall walk with shoulder taps: 3 x 5 each hand
- [ ] Freestanding handstand kick-up attempts: 5 attempts

> [!info]- Handstand Walk Progressions — Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Why handstand work?**
> Handstand walks are a secondary goal (gymnastics unlocks). Wall-facing holds and shoulder taps build the shoulder stability and body awareness needed for freestanding handstands. This is long-term skill development — don't expect immediate results. Consistency over weeks is what builds this.
>
> **Wall-facing handstand hold 3x30 sec**
> Face the wall, walk your feet up until your body is nearly vertical, hands close to the wall. Hold. Your body should be in a straight line — squeeze your glutes, point your toes, push the floor away with your hands. If 30 sec is easy, move your hands closer to the wall. Think about stacking: wrists under shoulders, shoulders over hips, hips over toes.
>
> **Wall walk with shoulder taps 3x5 each hand**
> From the wall-facing hold, shift your weight to one hand and tap the opposite shoulder with your free hand. This forces you to balance on one arm and develops the lateral weight shift needed for walking. Go slow — it's about control, not speed. If you wobble or fall off, the set still counts. The attempt is the training stimulus.
>
> **Freestanding kick-up attempts x5**
> Kick up to a handstand away from the wall. Try to hold for even 1-2 seconds. If you can't hold at all, focus on kicking up to vertical (not over-kicking). Over the weeks, hold time will gradually increase. Don't spend more than 5 attempts — this is practice, not testing.
>
> **Athlete-specific notes**
> Handstand work is knee-friendly since there's zero knee loading. After a week of squats, snatches, and step-downs, your knees get a welcome break here. Use this time to decompress mentally too — it's the end of the week.

## Accessory Window (15-20 min)

- [ ] Peterson step-downs: 3 x 10 each leg
- [ ] Pigeon stretch: 2 x 45 sec each side
- [ ] 90/90 hip rotation: 2 x 8 each side

> [!info]- Accessory — Mechanism & Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Peterson Step-Downs 3x10 each leg**
> Same as Monday. This appears twice per week (Mon and Fri) because patellar tendon rehab needs consistent, frequent loading. Left leg first, match on right. If Monday's felt easy at bodyweight, add a light DB (5 kg). The key is the same: 3-second controlled descent, left knee tracking straight over your toes, no dropping. This is the single most targeted exercise for your left knee patellar tracking issue — it specifically loads the VMO that controls kneecap alignment.
>
> **Pigeon Stretch 2x45 sec each side**
> Targets the deep external rotators of the hip (piriformis, etc.). Tight hips limit squat depth and can cause the knees to cave inward. Get into a pigeon position (front shin across your body, back leg extended behind), keep hips square, and fold forward until you feel a deep stretch in the front hip. Breathe and relax into it. Don't force depth — gravity and time do the work.
>
> **90/90 Hip Rotation 2x8 each side**
> Sit on the floor with both legs at 90-degree angles. Rotate your legs to switch which hip is externally rotated and which is internally rotated. This builds active hip mobility — the ability to move through full hip range under control. Note if one direction feels significantly tighter — that's likely the side that limits your squat symmetry. Move slowly and own each position for a beat before rotating.
