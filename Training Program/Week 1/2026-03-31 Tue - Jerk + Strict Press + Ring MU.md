> [!info]- Warm-up Movement Guide (click to expand)
>
> **Banded Lat Stretch x 45 sec each**
> Attach a band overhead, grip it with one hand, and step away until you feel a deep stretch through your lat and the side of your ribcage. Let your torso rotate slightly toward the band to open up the thoracic spine. Tight lats pull your elbows down in the front rack and limit how far overhead you can press — this directly addresses your thoracic restriction. Breathe into the stretch; 45 seconds is long enough for the tissue to actually yield.
>
> **Front Rack Stretch with Barbell x 60 sec**
> Set a barbell in a rack at front rack height. Step in and place the bar across your front delts with a clean grip. Now push your elbows up as high as you can and hold. The barbell provides a fixed point to stretch against — your wrists, lats, triceps, and thoracic spine all get loaded into the exact position you need for cleans and jerks. This is your most important warm-up drill given your left arm's front rack restriction. If your left elbow is noticeably lower than your right, spend extra time there.
>
> **Thoracic Rotation on Wall x 8 each side**
> Stand sideways to a wall, inside arm against the wall at shoulder height. Rotate your torso away from the wall, reaching your outside arm behind you. This mobilizes thoracic rotation, which is often locked up when extension is limited. Better rotation = better ability to get your elbows through in the front rack and maintain an upright torso in the jerk dip. Go slowly — find the sticky point and breathe into it.
>
> **Push Press with Empty Bar x 10**
> Dip and drive with the empty bar. This grooves the exact motor pattern you'll load up in the push jerk — vertical dip, aggressive leg drive, fast lockout. Use this to rehearse: elbows forward in the dip, torso vertical (not leaning forward), drive through the heels, punch the bar to lockout. 10 reps is enough to wake up the pattern without fatigue.
>
> **Jerk Footwork Drills x 10**
> Without a bar, practice your jerk receiving position. From a standing position, dip and drive your feet into your split or power jerk stance. Focus on: front shin vertical, back knee slightly bent, torso directly under where the bar would be. Repeat 10 times. This trains the feet to move fast and land in the right place — the most common jerk miss is not a strength issue, it's the feet landing wrong.

> [!info]- Push Jerk — Mechanism & Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Why push jerk?** The push jerk is the foundation of your jerk development. Before we add complexity (split jerk timing, foot positioning), you need a reliable dip-drive. The push jerk isolates the two things that matter most: a vertical dip and an aggressive drive into lockout. At 70%, the weight is light enough that you should never be grinding — if you're grinding, something positional is wrong.
>
> **The Dip — where most jerks are won or lost:**
> - Feet hip-width, toes slightly out (same stance you'd jump from)
> - Dip straight down — knees push forward over toes, torso stays vertical
> - Depth: only 4-6 inches. A shallow, fast dip is better than a deep, slow one
> - **Critical for your left knee:** the dip loads the patellar tendon in a knee-forward position. If you feel pain, first check that your left foot isn't turned in — slight toe-out (15-20 degrees) opens the joint and reduces patellar stress. If pain persists, slow the dip to a 2-second descent and/or drop the load 5-10%
> - **Critical for your thoracic restriction:** if your thoracic is tight, your elbows will drop during the dip, the bar slides forward off your shoulders, and you end up pressing it out in front. Cue: "elbows up and forward" throughout the dip. If you can feel the bar shifting forward, stop and re-rack — that rep is already compromised
>
> **The Drive:**
> - From the bottom of the dip, reverse direction explosively — think "jump the bar off your shoulders"
> - As the bar leaves your shoulders, push your head through (ears in front of arms)
> - Lock out aggressively — punch up hard. A soft lockout at 84 kg becomes a missed lockout at 110 kg
> - Receive with knees slightly bent (quarter squat), then stand
>
> **At 84 kg (70% of ~120 kg jerk):**
> This should feel moderate-light. RPE 5-6. If it feels heavier than that, the issue is positional, not strength. Film from the side — check that the bar travels straight up, not forward. The bar path tells you everything about your dip quality.

> [!info]- Strict Press — Mechanism & Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Why strict press in an Oly program?** The strict press builds the raw overhead strength and stability that underpins your jerk lockout. When you catch a heavy jerk, your shoulders, triceps, and upper back have to stabilize the bar in a locked-out position — the strict press is the most direct way to strengthen that exact demand. No leg drive means pure upper body — it exposes any imbalances or weaknesses you can hide with momentum.
>
> **Connection to your limiters:**
> - Your thoracic restriction directly affects bar path. If your upper back is rounded, the bar has to travel forward and around your head instead of straight up. You'll compensate by leaning back excessively — that's lower back stress, not shoulder strength. Cue: "ribs down, squeeze glutes" to prevent over-arching.
> - Watch for left-right asymmetry. If your left arm fatigues faster or locks out slower, that's a real signal about overhead stability — the same side with your thoracic restriction.
>
> **Execution tips:**
> - Start with the bar at your front rack (on the shoulders, elbows slightly in front of the bar)
> - Squeeze your glutes and brace your core before every rep — your body is the platform, and a wobbly platform leaks force
> - Press straight up. As the bar passes your forehead, push your head *through* so the bar ends up directly over the back of your head, over your spine
> - Full lockout: elbows completely straight, actively push up into the bar, shoulders shrugged slightly toward your ears
> - Lower under control back to your shoulders — don't just drop it
>
> **At ~60 kg (moderate):**
> Start here and see how it feels. This should be RPE 6-7 for sets of 6. If it's too easy (RPE < 5), bump to 63-65 kg for the remaining sets. If set 1 feels like RPE 8+, drop to 55 kg. The goal is quality reps, not grinding.

## Gymnastics Skill: Ring Muscle-Up Progressions (5-10 min)

- [x] Deep ring dips: 3 x 5 (slow negative) ✅ 2026-03-31
- [x] False grip hang: 3 x 15 sec ✅ 2026-03-31
- [x] Banded ring MU transitions: 3 x 3 ✅ 2026-03-31

> [!info]- Ring Muscle-Up Progressions — Mechanism & Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Why this progression?** Ring muscle-ups require three things: pulling strength into a deep position, the false grip to maintain contact through the transition, and the pressing strength to push out of the bottom. We build each component separately before combining them. You're not expected to get a muscle-up in Week 1 — this is building the foundation.
>
> **Deep Ring Dips: 3 x 5 (slow negative)**
> This targets the hardest part of the muscle-up — the bottom of the dip, where your shoulders are below your elbows and the rings want to flare out. A slow negative (3 seconds down) builds strength in the exact range where most people fail. Keep the rings turned out at the top (palms facing forward) and close to your body throughout. If you can't control the descent for 3 seconds, you're not ready for banded transitions yet — that's fine, it means the dips are doing their job.
>
> **False Grip Hang: 3 x 15 sec**
> The false grip is non-negotiable for strict ring muscle-ups. Your wrist sits on top of the ring (not hanging from your fingers), which shortens the pull distance and lets you transition over the rings without re-gripping. It feels awkward and tiring at first — that's normal. Hook your wrists over the rings so your palms face down, keep your wrists flexed, and just hang. If 15 seconds is easy, try a slight pull-up from the false grip position. If it's hard, it means your forearm flexors need this work.
>
> **Banded Ring MU Transitions: 3 x 3**
> Loop a band from the rings to your feet or knees. Pull from the false grip, and as your chest reaches ring height, aggressively lean forward over the rings and drive your hands down to your hips. This "sit-up" motion through the transition is the skill component — you're teaching your body the movement path. The band takes away enough weight to let you practice the pattern. Cue: "pull high, then sit up fast." If you're pulling to your chest but can't get over, the issue is usually not pulling high enough or not committing to the forward lean.
>
> **Progression tracking:** Note which component is the limiter — grip strength (false grip fails), pull height (can't get chest to rings), or transition (can pull high but can't get over). This tells us what to emphasize in coming weeks.

## Accessory Window (15-20 min)

- [x] Banded front rack stretch: 2 x 45 sec each side ✅ 2026-03-31
- [x] Prone Y-T-W raises: 3 x 8 each position ✅ 2026-03-31
- [x] External rotation with band: 3 x 12 each arm ✅ 2026-03-31

> [!info]- Accessory — Mechanism & Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Banded Front Rack Stretch: 2 x 45 sec each side**
> Attach a band at elbow height, grip it, and step away to create tension pulling your elbow up and back. Then get into a front rack position with the band pulling your elbow into a deeper position than you could achieve alone. This directly targets the lat and tricep tightness that limits your front rack — especially on your left side. You should feel the stretch through your lat and the back of your tricep. Spend extra time on the left if it's notably tighter. Breathe and relax into it — fighting the stretch defeats the purpose.
>
> **Prone Y-T-W Raises: 3 x 8 each position**
> Lie face down on a bench or the floor. Arms hanging down.
> - **Y:** Raise arms overhead at a 45-degree angle (forming a Y shape), thumbs up. This hits lower traps — the muscles that upwardly rotate your shoulder blades for overhead stability.
> - **T:** Raise arms straight out to the sides (forming a T), thumbs up. This targets mid-traps and rhomboids — the muscles that retract your shoulder blades and prevent forward rounding.
> - **W:** Elbows bent at 90 degrees, rotate arms up (forming a W). This targets external rotators and rear delts.
>
> These muscles are the foundation of a stable overhead position. If they're weak, your shoulders roll forward under load and your lockout suffers. Use no weight or very light dumbbells (1-3 kg). This is activation and endurance, not strength work — if you need heavy weight to feel it, your form is wrong.
>
> **External Rotation with Band: 3 x 12 each arm**
> Hold a band in front of you at elbow height, elbow pinned to your side at 90 degrees. Rotate your forearm outward against the band. This strengthens the infraspinatus and teres minor — the rotator cuff muscles that keep your shoulder joint healthy under pressing and overhead load. Do these every pressing day. It's prehab, not rehab — the goal is to keep your shoulders healthy as jerk and press volume climbs over the coming weeks. Control the return (don't let the band snap your arm back in).
