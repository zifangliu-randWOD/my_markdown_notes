## Warm-up (10 min)

- [ ] Hip circles
- [ ] Banded good mornings x 15
- [ ] Clean grip deadlift to knee x 5
- [ ] Hang muscle clean x 5
- [ ] Front squat with empty bar x 5

> [!info]- Warm-up Movement Guide (click to expand)
>
> **Hip Circles**
> General hip prep for the deep clean receiving position. Stand on one leg, draw large circles with the other knee — 10 forward, 10 backward, each side. This opens up the hip capsule in all directions and primes the joint for the deep squat catch position you'll need today. Don't rush — bigger, controlled circles are better than fast sloppy ones.
>
> **Banded Good Mornings x 15**
> Loop a band under your feet and behind your neck. Hinge at the hips, pushing them back, until you feel a strong hamstring stretch, then drive back up by squeezing your glutes. This activates the posterior chain (hamstrings, glutes, spinal erectors) — the primary movers for the clean pull and the deadlift. Keep your back flat throughout; if it rounds, the band tension is too high.
>
> **Clean Grip Deadlift to Knee x 5**
> Take a clean-width grip on an empty bar. Pull from the floor to just above the knee, then lower back down. This rehearses the first pull position — same back angle, same grip width, same weight distribution through the foot that you'll use when loaded. Focus: shins clear the bar by pushing the knees back, shoulders stay over the bar, back angle doesn't change. This is a position check, not a strength exercise.
>
> **Hang Muscle Clean x 5**
> From the hang position (bar at mid-thigh), extend the hips and pull the bar up, then whip the elbows through to catch it on the shoulders — no squat. This grooves the turnover and elbow speed without having to worry about catching deep. The muscle clean isolates the third pull (getting the elbows through) and directly tests your front rack mobility. If your left elbow is noticeably lower than your right on the catch, that's the thoracic restriction showing up — note it but don't force it.
>
> **Front Squat with Empty Bar x 5**
> Full depth front squat with a clean grip. This primes the receiving position for squat cleans and checks how your front rack feels today. Elbows high, torso vertical, full depth. If the left elbow drops or you feel restriction in the left wrist/lat, spend an extra 30 seconds in the bottom position with elbows driven up. This is your daily front rack status check.

> [!info]- Power Clean + Squat Clean Complex — Mechanism & Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Why this complex?** The power clean forces you to pull high and finish the extension. If you can't power clean it, your pull isn't complete — you're cutting the extension short and trying to get under too early. The squat clean that follows practices receiving deep. Doing both back to back teaches you the difference between pulling high (power) and pulling under (squat). This is the exact same principle as Monday's snatch complex but applied to the clean — building positional awareness at submaximal load.
>
> **Connection to your limiters:**
> The front rack catch position directly tests your thoracic mobility. The left arm will be the indicator — does the left elbow sit lower than the right? Does the bar shift left or feel uneven on your shoulders? At 98 kg (70%), the weight is light enough that you should focus entirely on elbow speed in the turnover. If your elbows are slow, the bar crashes onto you and the catch feels heavy — that's not a strength problem, it's a turnover speed problem.
>
> **Execution — Power Clean (rep 1):**
> - Set your clean grip (typically just outside the shoulders), hook grip
> - First pull (floor to knee): slow and controlled. Push the floor away with your legs. Shoulders stay over the bar, back angle constant, shins clear the bar
> - Second pull (knee to hip): accelerate. Sweep the bar into your hip crease. When contact happens, extend hard — jump the bar off your hips
> - Third pull (turnover): as the bar reaches max height, pull your elbows through fast — whip them around and up. The bar should land on your shoulders with you in a quarter-squat or power position
> - The bar should land cleanly on your front rack with minimal star-fishing (feet shouldn't splay wide)
>
> **Execution — Squat Clean (rep 2):**
> - Reset at the floor, same setup
> - Same first and second pull as the power clean
> - The difference is the third pull: instead of pulling the bar high and catching in a quarter squat, you pull yourself *under* the bar into a full front squat receiving position
> - The pull doesn't need to be as high — you're meeting the bar lower. Think "fast elbows and fast feet" as you drop under
> - Catch in a full depth front squat, pause for a beat to confirm stability, then stand
>
> **At 98 kg (70% of 140 kg clean):**
> This should feel moderate — RPE 5-6. The power clean should be comfortable, the squat clean even more so (since you're catching it deeper with less pull required). If the catch feels heavy or jarring, the issue is likely elbow speed in the turnover, not pull strength. Film from the side if you can — watch for whether the bar loops forward away from your body during the turnover.

| 4   | 150 kg | /5   |     |       |

> [!info]- Clean-Grip Deadlift (Tempo) — Mechanism & Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Why clean-grip deadlifts?** This strengthens the exact pulling position used in cleans. The clean grip (narrower than conventional) forces more quad engagement and a more upright pulling position compared to a conventional deadlift. You're not training "deadlift strength" generically — you're training the specific back angle, grip width, and pulling mechanics that directly transfer to your clean.
>
> **Why tempo (3 up, 3 down)?** Tempo builds positional awareness — you can't rush through weak points. If your back rounds at a certain height, you'll feel it during a 3-second pull. If you shift to your toes or let the bar drift forward, 3 seconds of exposure makes it obvious. The 3-second descent teaches you to maintain your back angle on the way down, which directly transfers to controlled lowering between reps in clean sets and eccentric loading for tendon adaptation.
>
> **Connection to your limiters:**
> - For the left knee: the clean-grip deadlift is relatively knee-friendly since it's more hip-dominant than squatting. Your knee will be loaded in the start position (knees bent, shins angled), but the movement is primarily a hip hinge. This should not aggravate patellar tracking the way squatting does.
> - For pulling mechanics: this builds the patience and positional discipline needed off the floor in cleans. If you rush the first pull in a clean, you'll do the same here — but 3 seconds makes rushing impossible.
>
> **Execution tips:**
> - Set up with a clean-width grip (hook grip optional — straps are fine for tempo deadlifts if grip is a limiter)
> - Feet hip-width, toes slightly out, shins touching the bar
> - 3-second pull: initiate by pushing the floor away. Count "one-thousand, two-thousand, three-thousand" from floor to lockout. Consistent speed — don't accelerate at the top
> - Lockout: hips fully extended, shoulders back, knees straight. Stand tall
> - 3-second descent: reverse the pull. Hips push back first, bar travels down your thighs, then past your knees to the floor. Same 3-second count. Maintain your back angle — don't just drop it
> - Reset briefly at the floor (touch and go is fine as long as you don't bounce)
>
> **At 150 kg (75% of 200 kg deadlift):**
> This is moderate-challenging. RPE 6-7. The tempo makes it significantly harder than regular 150 kg pulls — each set of 5 takes about 30 seconds of continuous tension. If it feels easy, the weight is correct — focus on perfecting the position.
>
> **Important note:** This is 75% of your deadlift 1RM, not squat 1RM. Given Monday's experience with tempo squats being harder than expected, be alert for the same pattern here. The same tempo that made 133 kg squats feel like RPE 8-9 will add significant difficulty to these pulls. If RPE hits 8+ by set 2, drop to 140 kg. There is no value in grinding tempo work — the point is positional quality, not survival.

## Accessory Window (15-20 min)

- [ ] Bulgarian split squat: 3 x 8 each leg (DB or KB)
- [ ] Single-leg RDL: 3 x 8 each leg
- [ ] Banded hip flexor stretch: 2 x 45 sec each side

> [!info]- Accessory — Mechanism & Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Bulgarian Split Squat: 3 x 8 each leg**
> The single best exercise for exposing and correcting left-right quad/glute asymmetry. Each leg works independently — you'll immediately feel which side is weaker. This directly addresses the left knee issue by strengthening the left quad/VMO in a single-leg context where the right leg can't compensate.
>
> *How to do it:* Stand about 2 feet in front of a bench, facing away. Place the top of your rear foot on the bench behind you. Hold a dumbbell in each hand at your sides (start light — 12-15 kg each hand). Descend by bending your front knee until your back knee nearly touches the floor. Your front knee tracks over your toes — don't let it cave inward. Drive back up through your front heel.
>
> **Always do left leg first** (the weaker side) and match reps on right. This prevents the right leg from setting a standard the left can't meet. If you can only complete 6 reps on the left, do 6 on the right — don't do 8 on the right and pretend it's even.
>
> For the left knee: slight toe-out on the front foot (15-20 degrees) reduces patellar stress. If you feel patellar pain, check foot position first. Depth should be challenging but pain-free — if pain appears at full depth, stop 2 inches higher.
>
> **Single-Leg RDL: 3 x 8 each leg**
> Trains hamstring and glute on one leg at a time, plus balance and hip stability. This exposes hip stability differences between left and right and strengthens the posterior chain unilaterally — the muscles that drive the clean pull and protect the knee joint.
>
> *How to do it:* Stand on one leg, hold a dumbbell in the opposite hand (contralateral loading). Hinge forward at the hip, keeping your back flat, reaching the dumbbell toward the floor while your free leg extends behind you as a counterbalance. You should feel a deep hamstring stretch on the standing leg. Reverse the movement by driving your hips forward and squeezing your glute to stand tall.
>
> If balance is a major limiter, lightly touch a wall or rack with your free hand — but only for balance, not support. The instability is part of the exercise. Start with a moderate dumbbell (10-15 kg). Left leg first, match reps on right.
>
> **Banded Hip Flexor Stretch: 2 x 45 sec each side**
> Tight hip flexors pull your pelvis into anterior tilt, which limits your ability to stay upright in the squat catch position and puts more stress on the lower back during pulls. This is recovery and mobility work, not strength.
>
> *How to do it:* Attach a heavy band to a low rig point behind you. Step into the band so it sits in your hip crease. Step forward into a half-kneeling position (back knee on the ground, front foot flat). The band pulls your hip forward and down, creating traction in the hip joint. Squeeze the glute on the kneeling side and lean slightly forward until you feel a deep stretch in the front of your hip. Hold, breathe into it, let the tissue release.
>
> Do both sides even if one feels tighter. If one side is notably tighter, that's useful information — note it in your log.

> [!note] Coach's Review — Day 3 (Box Session)
>
> **Clean — Hang Power Clean + Power Clean Complex**
> Top set of 110 kg hang power clean + power clean at RPE 8+ is a meaningful data point. The hang position removes the first pull advantage — there's no stretch reflex from the floor, and you have to catch in a power (quarter-squat) position rather than a deep squat receive. Hang power cleans are typically capped around 65–75% of a full clean max, so hanging at 110 kg (79% of your 140 kg clean) at RPE 8+ reflects solid hip extension and turnover speed. The fact that it felt strong rather than a survival grind is a better sign than the raw number.
>
> The progression was well-paced: 80 → 90 → 100 → 110, each step of 10 kg with RPE rising cleanly by about 1 point per jump. No red flags in the build — no sudden RPE spike that would suggest a technique breakdown under load. At 100 kg (RPE 8), you were still 10 kg from your top set, which means the 110 was aggressive but not reckless.
>
> **Deadlift — Conventional + Below-Knee Paused**
> 140 kg is 70% of your 200 kg deadlift max. RPE 8 for 2 x 5 at 70% is slightly elevated compared to a true fresh baseline (typically RPE 6–7), but this came after 6 sets of clean complexes — carry-over fatigue from the posterior chain work in the cleans is expected. The fact that it still "felt strong" is the key detail; RPE 8 here is a fatigue-adjusted reading, not a ceiling.
>
> Below-knee paused deadlifts (2 x 2 @ 130 kg → 2 x 2 @ 140 kg, RPE 8) add a layer of difficulty on top of the positional challenge: the pause eliminates momentum and forces you to maintain tension at the most mechanically disadvantaged point in the pull. Starting at 130 kg and building to 140 kg was the right call — the pause makes the effective load feel significantly heavier than the number suggests. Holding RPE 8 rather than 9+ at 140 kg with a pause, late in the session, is a solid indicator of posterior chain capacity and positional discipline at the sticking point.
>
> **Week 1 Pattern**
> Feeling strong in Week 1 is the ideal outcome. Early in a block, athletes often feel heavy or flat as the body adapts to new loading structure. Reporting "easy" and "strong" at these weights suggests you're entering Phase 1 with good base capacity — the program's submaximal volume will build on that without immediately grinding you down. Watch whether this carries into next week or was a one-off box session feeling.
