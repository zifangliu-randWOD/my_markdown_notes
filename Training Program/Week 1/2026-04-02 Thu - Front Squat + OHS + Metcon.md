# Thursday — Week 1: Front Squat + OHS + Metcon

[[00 - Program Overview]] | [[Phase 1 - Weeks 1-4]]

> [!tip] Day Focus
> Thursday is **front squat + overhead day** — the two positions most limited by your thoracic mobility. Front rack demands elbows high; overhead squat demands active shoulders and full thoracic extension. At 70%, front squats should be about maintaining an upright torso, not grinding. Based on Monday's experience with tempo back squats (133 kg was RPE 8-9 with failed reps), be ready to adjust weight down if 112 kg at tempo feels too heavy — drop to 95-100 kg without hesitation. The metcon is short and light; don't let it compromise recovery for Friday's Oly session.

## Warm-up (10 min)

- [ ] Thoracic foam roll
- [ ] Front rack stretch
- [ ] Goblet squat x 10
- [ ] OHS with empty bar x 5
- [ ] Snatch grip push press behind neck x 5

> [!info]- Warm-up Movement Guide (click to expand)
>
> **Thoracic Foam Roll**
> Same drill as Monday — roll mid-back only, roller perpendicular to your spine, hands behind head, extend back and exhale over it. Move one vertebra at a time from mid-back to upper back, 1-2 min. Today this is even more important than Monday because *both* primary lifts demand thoracic extension: front squat requires it to keep elbows high, and OHS requires it to hold the bar overhead without it drifting forward.
>
> **Front Rack Stretch**
> Set a barbell in a rack at shoulder height. Step into a front rack position and let the bar sit on your shoulders with elbows driven as high as possible. Let the weight of the bar push your elbows up while you relax into it. Hold 20-30 seconds, shake out, repeat 2-3 times. This directly prepares the front squat catch position. Pay extra attention to the left arm — it's more restricted and will resist the stretch more. Don't force it; just let gravity and time work.
>
> **Goblet Squat x10**
> Hold a kettlebell or dumbbell at your chest with both hands. Squat deep — elbows inside your knees, chest tall, weight in mid-foot. This is a front-loaded squat pattern that grooves the upright torso position needed for front squats without the front rack demand on your wrists and shoulders. Go light (16-20 kg) and focus on depth and position.
>
> **OHS with Empty Bar x5**
> Full depth overhead squat. Active shoulders (push up into the bar), midline tight, knees tracking over toes. This activates overhead stability for the OHS work later and serves as a mobility check — if this feels restricted, spend more time on the foam roll before loading up.
>
> **Snatch Grip Push Press Behind Neck x5**
> Hold the empty bar behind your neck in a snatch-width grip. Dip and drive, press to full lockout overhead. This grooves the overhead position from behind the neck, opening up the shoulders and upper back for OHS. Keep the bar path straight — if it drifts forward, your thoracic extension is insufficient and you should foam roll more.

> [!info]- Front Squat — Mechanism & Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Why front squats?** The front squat is THE lift that ties together squat strength and the receiving position for cleans. Every heavy clean is received in a front squat. If your front squat is weak or your position breaks down under load, you can't clean heavy — period. At 160 kg with a target of 170+ kg, there's clear room to grow. Building this in Phase 1 with tempo work creates positional strength that pays off when loads get heavier in Phase 2.
>
> **Connection to your limiters:**
> Front squats with tempo directly challenge your thoracic restriction. As you fatigue, the tendency is for elbows to drop and your torso to pitch forward — this is where cleans get missed. The 3-second descent amplifies this because you have to actively fight to keep elbows high for the entire descent rather than relying on momentum. Your left elbow will want to drop first due to the bilateral thoracic restriction being worse on the left. Cue on every rep: **"elbows UP."**
>
> **Left knee note:**
> Front squats have a more vertical torso and knees-forward position than back squats. This loads the patellar tendon more directly. If left knee pain appears:
> 1. Check foot position — slightly more toe-out (15-25 degrees) can relieve patellar tracking stress.
> 2. If pain persists after foot adjustment, reduce load before reducing reps.
> 3. The tempo is 3-0-0 (3 sec down, no pause, drive up) — easier than Monday's 3-1-0 back squat tempo which had a dead-stop pause. That means 70% *might* be manageable here. But given Monday's experience: if set 1 feels like RPE 8+, drop to 95-100 kg. Don't ego lift — this is position work.
>
> **How to execute:**
> - Set up with the bar in a clean grip (or cross-arm grip if front rack is too restricted today). Elbows as high as possible, upper arms parallel to the floor.
> - Big breath at the top, brace core hard (360-degree pressure).
> - 3 seconds down: count "one-thousand, two-thousand, three-thousand." Consistent speed all the way down — don't slow at the top and drop at the bottom.
> - No pause at the bottom — immediately drive up. Push the elbows up first as you stand.
> - Each set of 6 at tempo takes ~20-25 seconds. Stay braced the entire time.
>
> **Expected RPE at 112 kg:** Target RPE 6-7 per set. If set 1 is RPE 8+, that's the signal to drop weight.

> [!info]- Overhead Squat — Mechanism & Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Why OHS?** The overhead squat is the bottom position of the snatch. Every overhead squat you do under control builds the exact stability you need to catch snatches deeper and more confidently. Your snatch-to-squat ratio is 55% (105/190) — positional improvements are the biggest lever for snatch progress, and OHS drills that position directly. At only 60% of snatch (63 kg), this is pure position work — strength is not the limiter here.
>
> **Connection to your limiters:**
> OHS is the ultimate combined test of thoracic extension + shoulder stability + ankle mobility. If any one of these is lacking, the squat breaks down. Your thoracic restriction will show up as the bar drifting forward as you descend — you'll feel yourself fighting to keep the bar over mid-foot. The left side will be worse. Cues: **"push up into the bar"** and **"chest through."**
>
> **How to execute:**
> - Snatch grip on the bar, press or snatch it overhead.
> - Active shoulders: don't just hold the bar overhead passively. Actively push up into it throughout the entire squat. Shoulders should be shrugged slightly toward your ears (upper traps engaged). This is the exact demand of catching a snatch.
> - Descend under control — you don't need a strict tempo here, but don't rush. Full depth on every rep. No cutting it short.
> - At the bottom: bar should be directly over mid-foot (or slightly behind). If it's drifting forward, your thoracic is rounding. Press harder into the bar and drive your chest up.
> - Keep your core braced throughout. Midline collapse = bar drifting forward = missed snatches.
> - If your left arm bends or drifts forward at the bottom, that's the thoracic restriction showing up. Note it, but don't try to force through it — awareness is the first step.
>
> **Expected RPE at 63 kg:** RPE 4-5. This should feel light. Use the lightness to work on depth and position quality, not to zone out. Every rep is practice for catching a snatch.

> [!info]- Metcon — Strategy & Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Why a metcon in an Oly program?** Conditioning is #5 on the priority stack — maintained, not focused. Short metcons (12-15 min) keep your engine running without stealing recovery from the strength and Oly work that drives your primary goals. This workout tests legs (thrusters), pulling capacity (pull-ups), and aerobic base (run) in a simple, repeatable format.
>
> **43 kg thrusters:** About 23% of your front squat 1RM — very light. These should be unbroken or close to it. A thruster is NOT a front squat plus a press — use the dip-drive from the squat to throw the bar overhead in one fluid motion. Cycle efficiently: catch the bar in a partial front squat and immediately descend into the next rep. Don't muscle-press each one.
>
> **12 pull-ups:** Straightforward. If you can do 12 unbroken, do it. If not, break into manageable sets (7-5, 6-6, etc.) to avoid going to failure. Grip fatigue from pull-ups can carry into tomorrow's Oly session, so don't redline here.
>
> **200m run:** Easy pace. This is active recovery between rounds. Don't sprint — maintain a steady jog that lets your heart rate come down slightly before the next round of thrusters.
>
> **Pacing strategy:** Moderate and consistent. This is NOT the day to go all-out — you have Oly lifting tomorrow (Friday). Aim for consistent round splits rather than a blowout first round followed by a death march. A good time for this workout would be 8-11 minutes. Don't stress about the clock — focus on moving well through each round.
>
> **Left knee note:** Thrusters at 43 kg are very light, but they still involve a knees-forward squat pattern. If your left knee is talking after the front squat work, consider slightly wider stance on thrusters to reduce patellar load.

## Accessory Window (15-20 min)

- [ ] Thoracic extension over bench with barbell: 3 x 10 breaths
- [ ] Ankle dorsiflexion stretch (wall): 2 x 30 sec each side
- [ ] Banded overhead distraction: 2 x 30 sec each arm

> [!info]- Accessory — Mechanism & Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Thoracic Extension Over Bench with Barbell**
> This is the more aggressive version of Monday's foam roller thoracic work. The bench creates a sharper fulcrum, and the barbell overhead adds a gentle stretch that pulls your chest open further.
>
> *How to do it:* Lie face-up on the floor with your upper back draped over the edge of a bench (the bench edge sits across your mid/upper back). Hold an empty barbell overhead with straight arms in a moderate grip. Let gravity pull your chest open and thoracic spine into extension while the barbell weight creates a gentle overhead stretch. Take 10 full breaths per set — exhale completely and relax deeper into the stretch with each exhale. Don't force range; let time and breathing do the work.
>
> **Ankle Dorsiflexion Stretch (Wall)**
> *How to do it:* Face a wall, place one foot about 4 inches from the wall. Push your knee forward toward the wall, keeping your heel firmly planted on the floor. If your knee touches the wall easily, move your foot back an inch and try again. Hold when you find the point of stretch (not pain). This directly improves the knee-forward position needed for front squats and the catch position in cleans — if your ankles are stiff, your knees can't travel forward, which forces your torso to pitch forward to compensate. Do both ankles and note if one side is tighter (often the left, given the asymmetry pattern).
>
> **Banded Overhead Distraction**
> *How to do it:* Loop a heavy resistance band around a pull-up bar. Grip the band with one hand, step away so the band creates tension pulling your arm overhead and slightly back/outward. Let the band pull your arm into the overhead position while you relax into the stretch. This targets the lats and shoulder capsule in the exact overhead position needed for snatches and OHS. Spend extra time on the left shoulder if it feels tighter — this is the side where your thoracic restriction is worse, and the lat/shoulder capsule on that side is likely contributing to the restriction.
