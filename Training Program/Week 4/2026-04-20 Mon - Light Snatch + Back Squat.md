> **90/90 Breathing — 3 min (extended)**
> Lie on your back, feet flat on the wall, knees and hips at 90 degrees. Exhale fully through the mouth, flatten the lower back completely into the floor — no gap. Hold that position and take 3-4 slow nasal breaths per set, trying to breathe into the lower back (360-degree expansion). Do 2-3 sets of 4 breaths each. This protocol has been in place since Week 2 and is directly addressing your anterior pelvic tilt and lumbar stiffness — and as of Week 3 Thursday, it's working. The constraint at parallel depth in front squats has reportedly resolved. Continue the habit to consolidate the adaptation. Three minutes today instead of two because deload = more time to do it right.
>
> **Thoracic Foam Roll — 3 min**
> Roller perpendicular to the spine. Work from mid-back (T6-T7) up to the top of the thoracic spine (T1-T2). At each segment: hands behind head, elbows together, exhale and let gravity extend you over the roller. Hold 5 seconds, inhale, move one notch up. Your left side is stiffer — at each segment, let the left arm open slightly more than the right. You're not trying to crack anything; you're accumulating range over minutes of sustained loading. This directly improves your front rack position, jerk dip mechanics, and the overhead catch position for snatches.
>
> **Ankle Circles + Wall Ankle Stretch — 2 min each side**
> Ankle mobility feeds into squat depth and pulling mechanics. Stand facing the wall, one foot close (5-10 cm), drive the knee forward and touch the wall without the heel coming up. Work through the available range. Both sides, but pay attention to the left ankle — restricted ankle dorsiflexion on the left contributes to the knee tracking issues by forcing compensatory torso lean. 2 minutes per side, slow and intentional.
>
> **Hip 90/90 Stretch — 2 min each side**
> Seated on the floor, both knees bent at 90 degrees (one shin in front, one to the side). Sit tall, shift weight to the front hip, hold the stretch into the hip capsule. Then rotate to the other side. The goal is internal and external rotation range in the hip. Tight hip capsules directly limit the squat-receive position in the snatch. Two minutes per side allows passive tissue creep to kick in — you need at least 90 seconds for a meaningful change.
>
> **PVC Snatch Grip OHS — 10 reps (slow descent)**
> The PVC position check before you load anything. Snatch-width grip, bar pressed overhead. Descend slowly, pausing at 90 degrees and again at full depth. Watch for: (1) bar drifting forward, (2) chest collapsing, (3) heels coming up. Any of these = the segment that needs more attention in the foam roll or ankle stretch. Apply "knuckles to ceiling" — heel of hand pressing up, wrist slightly extended.
>
> **Snatch Balance with Empty Bar — 5 reps**
> Bar on back, dip and drive, punch fast under into full OHS. This is the best warm-up drill for the pulling-under reflex. At deload intensity (73 kg today), the snatch won't require a fast pull-under — the bar will practically float. But practicing the speed drill in the warm-up keeps the motor pattern sharp. Five reps, each one punched as fast as possible under the bar. Stick the catch — don't just reach for it.

> ---
>
> **The bar-forward failure pattern: what actually happened Friday and how to fix it**
>
> Friday's 3 missed reps (set 3 rep 1, set 6 reps 2-3) shared one failure mode: the bar fell forward out of the catch. You described it as the bar being "driven high" on every set — but under fatigue, "drive high" stops meaning "drive vertically" and starts meaning "drive forward-and-up." Here is the mechanical breakdown:
>
> When fatigue accumulates in the posterior chain (hamstrings, glutes, lower back), the second pull loses its vertical hip extension component. The hips don't complete their forward drive before the bar leaves contact with the body. Instead, the back extensors compensate and the bar path tilts forward. The bar ends up 5-10 cm in front of your base of support at the catch. No amount of "knuckles to ceiling" rescues a bar that's already forward — the overhead position has to fight gravity and the bar's momentum simultaneously.
>
> **The fix has two components:**
>
> 1. **Stay patient off the floor.** The bar-forward pattern often starts at the floor — if you rush the first pull and let the bar swing away from your legs below the knee, the second pull has no chance to stay vertical. Keep the bar dragging close to the shins on the way up. "Sweep the bar into the hip" is still the cue — it starts from the moment the bar leaves the floor.
>
> 2. **Finish the hip drive before pulling under.** This is the critical fix. The sequence must be: (a) full hip extension, hips forward and tall, body vertical — THEN (b) elbows high and outside, pull yourself under. When fatigued, the pull-under begins too early and the hip drive never completes. The bar floats forward because the body wasn't tall yet. At 73 kg, there is zero excuse for premature pull-under. The weight is light enough to afford patience. Practice extending FULLY before moving under the bar. You may feel the bar going higher than usual — that is correct.
>
> **Eye gaze protocol (established Week 3 Friday):**
> For the snatch receiving position — upright torso, deep squat — pick a spot on the wall at eye level, 2-3 meters ahead. Keep your eyes on that point from the pull all the way through the catch and stand-up. When eyes drop, chest follows. The "chest to the ceiling" cue on the second pull extension means: at full extension, your sternum is pointing up and slightly forward. If your chin drops, your chest will follow, and the bar path goes with it. Build this habit at light weight so it's automatic at 85+ kg in Phase 2.
>
> **No compensation sets.**
> Friday's set 6 was added to make up for the missed rep on set 5. It produced 2 more misses and reinforced the error pattern at RPE 9. The rule going forward: if a rep is missed, note it and move on. Today, at 73 kg, missed reps are informational — they mean something in your setup or timing is wrong at a weight that shouldn't be missable. Note what felt off and correct on the next set. Do not add sets.
>
> **Build-up reps before working sets:**
> 40 kg x 3 (empty bar feel, slow) → 55 kg x 2 → 65 kg x 1 → 70 kg x 1 → 73 kg working sets
>
> Each build-up rep: apply all cues. Don't treat the warm-up reps as throwaway sets. The 70 kg rep before your 73 kg sets is the last practice before the "real" reps — make it count.
>
> **What RPE ≤5 feels like at 73 kg:**
> 70% of your max should not challenge your ability to complete the lift. If it does, one of three things is happening: (1) accumulated fatigue is higher than expected — valid, and this is information, (2) technique is breaking down and you're fighting the bar, or (3) your true max is lower than 105 kg. Barring option 3 (unlikely given the Friday 84 kg single), RPE ≤5 at 73 kg should be achievable without effort. If reps feel like 6+, note it — recovery from Week 3 is the story of this week.
>
> **Athlete-specific notes:**
> - Left knee: the snatch from floor has minimal knee-forward loading during the receiving position — this is one of the safest movements for your patellar tracking issue. The vertical torso concern is more relevant to jerk dips and front squats. Expect no knee issues today.
> - Thoracic restriction: the overhead catch position at 73 kg won't stress your mobility ceiling. Use the lightness as an opportunity to practice a deep, stable catch with the bar directly over the midfoot — without fighting the weight, your body can find its best overhead position.
> - Anterior shoulder (new finding from Friday): the squat clean pull produced an anterior shoulder stretch Friday that wasn't present on power cleans. Today's snatches use a different path — watch for any similar sensation. If you feel a pull in the anterior shoulder during the pull-under, note it. This may be related to the bar path going slightly forward (which loads the anterior capsule as the arm reaches forward to catch a drifting bar).

> [!info]- Back Squat — Mechanism & Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Why 55% and only 3x5?**
> Week 3 peak: 5 x 5 at 110 kg (58%). Today is 3 x 5 at 105 kg (55%). Three fewer sets and 5 kg less. The goal is to keep the movement pattern alive, maintain tendon loading, and practice the technique fixes identified in Week 3 — not to accumulate fatigue. 105 kg at the tempo prescribed should feel like a moderate warm-up. If it feels heavy, that tells you how fatigued Week 3 left you.
>
> ---
>
> **Technique habit 1: Between-rep breathing (established Week 3 Monday, confirmed effective)**
>
> The breathing strategy fix was validated in Week 3: RPE dropped from 8-8.5 (Week 2, 115 kg) to 7-7.5 (Week 3, 110 kg) with this change alone. The sequence:
>
> 1. Stand at the top. Fully upright. Take 1-2 breaths to recover.
> 2. Big breath in, brace 360 degrees (pressure around the entire midsection, front and back), hold.
> 3. Descend for 3 seconds — bar moves, brace doesn't.
> 4. Pause 1 second at the bottom — still holding brace.
> 5. Drive up. Exhale through pursed lips past the sticking point.
> 6. Return to the top. Breathe again before the next rep.
>
> At 105 kg deload weight, you could probably just hold your breath across all 5 reps and survive. Don't. Use the lightness to make the correct breathing pattern automatic. When loads climb to 130-140 kg in Phase 2, the between-rep breath is what keeps RPE manageable. Build the habit now.
>
> **Do not breathe at the bottom.** Releasing the brace in the hole under load — even at 105 kg — puts shear stress on the lumbar spine. Not recommended.
>
> ---
>
> **Technique habit 2: Eye gaze (established Week 3 Monday, coach review)**
>
> In Week 3 Monday's session, you noted that looking up during the drive feels easier. The coach review established the correct protocol: **"chest up, eyes follow."** Here's the full breakdown for today:
>
> - **At the top and during descent:** pick a spot on the floor 2-3 meters ahead of you. Your gaze should land on the floor naturally — not at the wall, not at the ceiling. This keeps your neck in a neutral position that follows the torso angle.
> - **During the drive:** as your torso rises, your gaze naturally rises with it. Let it. Do NOT deliberately tilt your head up to look at the ceiling or wall. If you feel the urge to look up, it's a signal that your chest is dropping and your upper back is rounding — the compensatory head tilt is trying to pull the barbell path straight. The fix is to drive the chest up, not to look up.
> - **The test:** pick a spot on the floor at the start of each set and commit to it for all 5 reps. At 105 kg, this should be straightforward — there's no sticking point demanding compensation. If your gaze wanders upward, you'll notice it. Note whether the reps feel different from the Week 3 session where you were still experimenting.
>
> The goal by end of Week 4: the floor-gaze is automatic. No conscious decision required. You just look where your torso angle points.
>
> ---
>

> 55% x 190 kg = 104.5 kg → rounded to 105 kg (1.25 kg plates). If 105 kg isn't available as a clean plate combination, 102.5 kg is fine — the principle matters, not the exact number.

> **Peterson Step-Downs — 3 x 10 each leg (asymmetric protocol, maintained)**
>
> Deload rule: maintain, do not progress. Week 3 was 3 x 12 — today drops back to 3 x 10. This is intentional: the step-down is a VMO isolation movement and a direct rehabilitation exercise for the left knee patellar tracking issue. The deload is not an excuse to skip it; patellar tracking rehab accumulates over weeks of consistent practice, and missing a full deload week sets back the adaptation. Do all 10 reps on each leg.
>
> **Left leg (priority — do first):** 10-15 cm step height, 8-10 kg KB in the same-side hand. Lower slowly — 3-4 second eccentric — until the opposite heel barely touches the floor, then drive back up without pushing off the floor. Vertical torso throughout: do not lean forward, do not let the left knee cave inward. The VMO fires hardest in the terminal extension range — the last 30 degrees before lockout. Own that range on every rep. If 8 kg feels very easy, use 10 kg. If the left knee shows any pain or tracking sensation, drop to bodyweight and report in the daily log.
>
> **Right leg:** 20 cm step height, 8-10 kg KB. Higher step means greater range of motion and more eccentric demand. The right side is structurally stronger — use the higher step to keep it challenged without over-loading the left. The right-leg work also indirectly reinforces the bilateral comparison: if the right-side eccentric feels dramatically easier than the left at the same KB weight, that asymmetry is still present and should be noted.

> **What to track:** Does the left knee feel different after a full week of peak loading? Is there any lingering soreness or stiffness from Week 3 that affects the step-down? Log this — it feeds into the Week 4 deload reflection at the end of the week.

> **Spanish Squat Iso Hold — 3 x 30 sec**
>
> Dropped from 3 x 40 sec (Week 3) to 3 x 30 sec — deload volume cut. The Spanish squat is a patellar tendon loading exercise: the band behind the knees creates a posterior force that keeps the tibia vertical and isolates the quad/patellar tendon more than a regular squat hold. The iso hold trains tendon tolerance to sustained compression, which is the direct adaptation needed for your left knee patellar tracking rehabilitation.
>
> Setup: light band behind the knees, band anchored to a low anchor point or held by a partner. Lean back slightly to keep the shins vertical, settle into a quarter-squat depth, hold. The burn should be in the quads (thighs), not behind the kneecap. If you feel it behind the kneecap, raise the depth until the sensation moves to the muscle belly. Hold 30 seconds, rest 60 seconds between sets.
>
> At deload intensity, 30 seconds should be genuinely challenging (RPE 5-6 on the hold) but recoverable within 60 seconds. If it's not challenging by week's end, the tendon adaptation is progressing — good.

> [!note] Coach's Review — Day 17 (Week 4 Monday)
>
> **Fatigue confirmed — right on schedule**
> RPE drift across both lifts tells the story: snatch climbing from 5.5 to 6.5 across three sets, back squat from 5.5 to 7. At true recovery, deload weights should produce flat RPE. Instead, the body is accumulating fatigue within the session, which means the nervous system is still carrying Week 3's load. This is expected and is exactly what Week 4 is designed to absorb. By Thursday and Friday, RPE at these weights should flatten out and start feeling genuinely light. If it doesn't, that's information.
>
> **Right lumbar stiffness — new and worth tracking**
> The previous lumbar issue was bilateral, central, and related to anterior pelvic tilt — and that resolved in Week 3. What's appearing today is right-side specific. Two likely mechanisms: (1) normal asymmetric DOMS from peak volume — right-dominant athletes accumulate slightly more load on the right side across pulling and squatting movements; (2) left hip ER stiffness from Saturday is still present, and the body is compensating by over-rotating through the right lumbar under load. These connect: restricted left hip external rotation → pelvis tilts slightly during the pull → right lumbar takes more rotational stress. Monitor through the deload. If it resolves by Friday with rest and the mobility work, it's DOMS. If it's still there in Week 5, investigate.
>
> **Flat-sole shoes: a real finding, worth a deliberate experiment**
> This is worth taking seriously, not dismissing. Here's the honest breakdown:
>
> Most competitive weightlifters use raised-heel shoes (typically 18-22 mm) because the heel elevation effectively increases usable ankle range, allowing a more upright torso in the squat-receive without requiring that range from the ankle itself. For athletes with limited ankle dorsiflexion, heeled shoes are a significant performance enabler.
>
> But you've confirmed good ankle dorsiflexion — you can deep squat barefoot with a vertical torso. That means you're deriving less of a range benefit from the heel elevation than the average athlete. What the heel elevation *does* do for you is shift the load vector slightly forward. More anterior force = more anterior knee stress = potentially more patellar tracking aggravation. This is a plausible mechanism for why the left knee felt less stressed in flat shoes today.
>
> The "better ground feel" is also real: flat soles maximize the foot-floor contact area and proprioceptive feedback. Some athletes find this especially beneficial for the pull phase (floor to hip), where stability matters more than the heel-rise advantage.
>
> **Week 5 experiment** — try one snatch/clean session in flat shoes and note: (1) left knee feel, (2) receiving position stability and upright torso, (3) any difference in pull mechanics. If the receiving position holds up and the left knee response is consistently better, shifting to flat shoes for Olympic lifting is a legitimate option. Don't abandon weightlifting shoes entirely until you've tested the receive position deliberately — the heel rise does help in the catch, and you don't want to discover a problem there at 90 kg.
>

> No bar-forward failures today at 73 kg. This confirms what was suspected: Friday's misses were pure fatigue, not embedded technique problems. The motor patterns are intact. The deload is working as designed.
