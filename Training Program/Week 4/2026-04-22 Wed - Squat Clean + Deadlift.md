> [!info]- Squat Clean — Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Why squat cleans — specifically on this Wednesday**
> Every Wednesday in Weeks 1, 2, and 3 was a box session: hang power cleans and power cleans to max (120 kg Week 3 Wednesday). That's three consecutive Wednesdays with zero squat clean reps. The squat clean receiving position — catching the bar in a full front squat — is a distinct motor skill from the power clean, and it decays without practice. On Friday Week 3, the cost of that decay appeared clearly: at 98 kg in the C&J, the pull-under timing broke down. You described "almost forgetting how to catch the bar," and on set 4 the grip broke during the catch and the bar nearly hit your face. That is a pattern failure, not a strength failure. 98 kg is a load you can power clean easily. The problem was that the pull-high motor pattern from the box sessions bled into the squat clean pull-under — the bar was driven high (as in a power clean) and then the body tried to catch it late, from a compromised position.
>
> Today is the correction. 98 kg at 70% is comfortable territory — well below what you can pull. The training stimulus is not the weight; it is the receiving position, practiced deliberately, 6 reps, with a 2-second pause enforcing ownership of the catch.
>
> **Countering the pull-high bleed-over**
> The core problem from Friday's C&J was this: the box power clean pattern finishes with maximal bar height and a catch-and-settle — the bar goes as high as possible and you catch it wherever it lands. In a squat clean, you don't need the bar that high. You need the bar to rise only to chest height, then you pull yourself under it aggressively. These are different finishing intents. After three weeks of power clean sessions, the nervous system has been trained to drive the bar as high as possible. To counter this:
>
> - **Think "pull under," not "pull up."** At the moment the hips finish extending, your intent shifts immediately from "drive the bar" to "pull myself under." This is a mindset switch, not a timing cue.
> - **Elbows. Fast and early.** The earlier the elbows come through on the turnover, the lower the bar can be when you catch it — because you meet it faster. Late elbows = bar drives higher before you get under = power clean pattern. Fast elbows = bar meets you earlier = squat clean pattern.
> - **The bar doesn't need to get above your nipple line.** At 98 kg, if you pull it to your sternum and get under fast, that's a perfect squat clean. If it goes to your chin, you probably pulled it like a power clean.
>
> **The 2-second pause in the catch**
> Same protocol as Saturday. At the bottom of the front squat, count one-one-thousand, two-one-thousand. During the pause, run through the checklist:
> - Elbows? Should be horizontal or above, fingertips loose, bar resting on deltoid shelf.
> - Torso? Vertical. Not forward-lean. If your torso is leaning, your thoracic extension or front rack is failing — breathe and fight for vertical.
> - Eyes? On the wall at eye level. "Chest to the ceiling" — if you're looking at the floor, your chest is falling.
> - Bar position? On the shelf (deltoid/clavicle), not in the palms. If you're gripping hard with the hands, the front rack is the limiter.
> Stand only after the pause. Do not rush.
>
> **Anterior shoulder monitoring protocol — primary diagnostic test of this session**
> On Friday Week 3, you felt a stretch/pull sensation on the anterior shoulder joint during the squat clean pull-under. It was described as "a little sore, definitely feels like the joint is being pulled." Critically: this was NOT present on power cleans at 120 kg on Wednesday, which means the first and second pulls are not the cause. The difference is the third pull — pulling under the bar — where the arms internally rotate and the shoulder capsule is loaded as the bar clears the face. Combined with a forward bar path from the decayed pattern, the anterior capsule was likely reaching forward-and-up to catch a bar that wasn't close enough.
>
> Saturday at 85 kg did NOT reproduce the sensation — which suggests either a load-dependent mechanism or a pattern-dependent one. Today at 98 kg in fresh conditions is the decisive test.
>
> **What to feel for, rep by rep:**
> Before each pull, consciously note the state of the anterior shoulder — is there any baseline tightness or sensitivity? This is your control reading. During the pull-under (the moment you whip your elbows through and drop under the bar), note whether you feel any stretch, pull, or discomfort at the anterior shoulder joint — the crease between the front of the shoulder and the chest, or into the bicipital groove. This is the sensation you're looking for.
>
> **If it reproduces today (at 98 kg fresh):**
> Stop after the set where it appears. Note which rep, which set, and how intense (1-5 scale). Complete the remaining sets at 85 kg instead. Log it in the Daily Log with specific language. This is a bar-path plus pattern issue. For Week 5, the warm-up will gain band external rotation (3x15 each arm) and sleeper stretch (2x60 sec each arm) before the clean work. The pull-under mechanics also need drilling: bar-close hang power cleans (bar path emphasis) and tall cleans at empty bar.
>
> **If it does NOT reproduce today (at 98 kg fresh):**
> Continue all three sets as programmed. Log that it was absent. The most likely explanation in that case is that Friday's occurrence was fatigue-specific — a combination of peak-volume accumulated fatigue, a degraded catch pattern after 3 weeks without squat cleans, and a slightly forward bar path. Deload week cleared the fatigue; the positional practice restored the pattern enough that the shoulder isn't strained by the pull-under. Continue monitoring at higher loads when Week 5 ramps back up (105–110 kg range).
>
> **"Chest to the ceiling" cue for the second pull and catch**
> Wednesday Week 3 at the box showed a clear finding: under heavy loads (110+ kg), chest and eyes drop during the pull — the back angle collapses forward, the bar drifts away from the body, and the catch becomes a catch-and-collapse. This cue — "chest to the ceiling" — is specifically for the moment after the bar passes the knee: as you transition from the first pull to the second pull, actively fight to keep the chest up. The back angle should be constant or opening (becoming more vertical), never closing. At 98 kg this should feel easy — use it as deliberate practice of the cue, because the habit needs to be automatic at 120+ kg in Phase 2. Say it before each rep during the warm-up, then internalize it.
>
> **Wider stance experiment in the receiving position**
> Tuesday's jerk work found that a +5 cm wider stance improved front-foot feel and may have shifted center of mass more favorably. The question for today: does that same wider stance also feel more natural in the clean receiving position? Try it on set 2 or 3. The receiving position of a clean and the split position of a jerk are mechanically different (bilateral vs split), so the optimal stance widths may differ — but it's worth testing. Specifically note: does the wider stance make it easier or harder to hit full depth? Does the front squat at the bottom feel more stable or less? You're not committing to it — you're gathering data.
>
> **Expected RPE**
> Each set should feel like RPE 5–6. If any set is RPE 7+, something is wrong — either the pattern is still broken enough to make the lift inefficient, or the fatigue recovery from Week 3 is incomplete. Log it honestly. The deload is designed to have you feeling fresh at 70%.

> [!info]- Clean-Grip Deadlift — Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Why this weight feels easy — and why that's the point**
> 140 kg is 70% of your 200 kg deadlift. Week 3 Wednesday at the box you pulled 150–155 kg in clean-grip for 5x5 at RPE 8–9. Today's 3x3 at 140 kg with no tempo and full recovery is a deliberate contrast — the purpose is nervous system recovery and movement quality rehearsal, not stimulus. If this feels easy, it's working as designed.
>
> **Clean grip mechanics — use this as a position session**
> The clean-grip deadlift is specifically about building the pulling position that carries over to the clean. Unlike a conventional deadlift (shoulder-width hands), the wider clean grip lowers your hips slightly (to maintain bar proximity), raises your back angle (you're more upright), and demands more upper back rigidity to hold the bar close. At 140 kg with low RPE, cognitive bandwidth is available to focus on:
> - **Bar proximity.** The bar should graze your shins on the way up. If you're pulling forward and away, reset.
> - **Back angle.** Find it at the setup — hips down, shoulders over bar, back flat — and hold it constant through the first pull. This is the "chest to the ceiling" position.
> - **Shoulder engagement.** At the setup, before you pull, actively pull the shoulder blades together and down (scapular retraction + depression). This locks the upper back rigid. Then pull.
> - **Foot pressure.** Drive through the full foot — heel and ball — not just the toes. As the bar passes the knee, finish with a hard hip drive.
>
> **No tempo on deload deadlifts**
> Week 3's 5x5 at 150 kg used a 3-0-3 tempo — 3-second pull up, 3-second lower. That added significant effective load and was appropriate for peak volume week. Today's deload pulls have no tempo — just clean, controlled reps at a natural pace. Lower the bar under control (2 sec) to protect the lower back, but don't artificially slow the upward pull. Come back to tempo deadlifts in Week 5.
>
> **Lower back check**
> After three Wednesday deadlift sessions and peak-volume week, the lumbar region may be carrying some residual fatigue even 5 days post-Friday. 140 kg should not challenge the lower back, but if you notice any stiffness or discomfort during set 1, add a 90-second rest before set 2 and log it. If pain appears (distinct from stiffness), stop and note it — this is deload week and there is no reason to push through any discomfort.
>
> **Expected RPE**
> RPE 4–5 per set. The bar should move smoothly with no real effort on any rep. If it feels harder than that, check your setup — specifically your back angle and bar proximity. Poor position inflates the effective RPE at any weight.
