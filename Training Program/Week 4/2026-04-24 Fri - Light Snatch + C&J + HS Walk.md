## Warm-up (10 min)

- [ ] **90/90 breathing: 2 min** (feet on wall, flatten lower back into floor, 3-4 breath cycles per set)
- [ ] Snatch grip RDL: 5 reps
- [ ] Hang snatch high pull: 5 reps
- [ ] Snatch balance: 5 reps
- [ ] Clean & jerk with empty bar: 3 reps

> [!info]- Warm-up Movement Guide (click to expand)
>
> **90/90 Breathing — 2 min**
> Same pelvic reset that's been in place since Week 2. The lumbar-pelvic constraint that showed up under Thursday front squats in Weeks 2–3 has been resolving — the 90/90 breathing is part of why. Keep it as the opening ritual even on deload days. Today it also functions as a recovery status check: if the lower back still feels stiff heading into Week 5, that's information worth logging.
>
> **Snatch Grip RDL x 5**
> Builds hamstring awareness in the pulling position. Focus on keeping the bar close to the shins, shoulders over or slightly ahead of the bar, back flat. This is the first pull position — the one that sets the bar path for everything that follows. At snatch grip width, you should feel a deep hamstring load by 30–40% of hinge depth. If the bar drifts forward off the shins, your first pull is likely doing the same thing under load.
>
> **Hang Snatch High Pull x 5**
> From mid-thigh, extend hard through the hips, drive elbows up and out, bar rising to sternum height. This drills the violent hip extension that ends the second pull. The key cue: **elbows lead, hands follow.** The bar doesn't go up because your arms pull it — it goes up because your hips drive it and your elbows get out of the way fast. This is the mechanics that produced the 84 kg single in Week 3. At 73 kg today, it should feel effortless.
>
> **Snatch Balance x 5**
> Bar on back in a narrow snatch-width stance. Dip slightly, drive the bar up, and punch yourself under into a full OHS catch. This is the most specific warm-up drill for the turnover — the moment where bar height is already determined and you're just pulling yourself into the hole fast. Apply "knuckles to ceiling" on every catch: press up and out with the heel of your hand. At deload load today, the snatch balance is a pure reflex drill. Own the catch position.
>
> **Clean & Jerk with Empty Bar x 3**
> Full movement rehearsal. On the clean: pull from the floor, ride to full front squat depth, 1-second pause at the bottom — elbows high, torso vertical. On the jerk: dip with "heels heavy," drive vertically, lock out overhead. Use the wider jerk stance that has been the default since Week 3 (wider = +5 cm than your old narrow stance). If the anterior shoulder finding from Friday affected your shoulder warm-up this week, add 5–10 band pull-aparts at snatch grip width before the empty bar C&J.

> [!info]- Snatch — Mechanism & Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Where 73 kg sits in context:**
> Your current snatch PR is 105 kg. 73 kg is 69.5% — roughly the weight you were using for snatch complexes in Week 1. The difference is what you bring to it now: 4 weeks of "sweep into hip," 4 weeks of squat-receive cuing, 4 weeks of bar path awareness, and — most importantly — a 84 kg single at RPE 7 in Week 3. At 73 kg today, the bar should feel almost trivially light. That's the point. When the bar is light enough that technique is no longer challenged by weight, you can focus entirely on the motor pattern — and the motor pattern you groove at light weight is the one that shows up when the bar gets heavy.
>
> **The 84 kg single as calibration data:**
> 84 kg at RPE 7 means your technical ceiling with good positions is above 84 kg. An RPE 7 single means roughly 3 reps left in the tank — which puts your true 1RM (in good positions on a fresh day) closer to 91–95 kg. The gap between 91–95 kg technical ceiling and 105 kg PR is now about 10–15 kg — that gap lives in positions and timing, not in strength. This is what the program is built around. Today at 73 kg, every rep should reinforce that ceiling.
>
> **The bar-forward failure pattern — and the deload fix:**
> Week 3 Friday's working sets (80 kg, 5 sets) showed a consistent failure mode: bar falling forward out of the catch, especially under fatigue. Three missed reps, all the same pattern. The mechanism: when fatigued, the second pull loses its vertical component. Instead of finishing the hip drive (hips extend fully before the arms kick in), the pull starts early — the back takes over and the bar gets yanked slightly forward instead of straight up. By the time it's overhead, the bar is an inch or two in front of your center of mass, and no catch can recover that.
>
> At 73 kg on fresh legs, this failure mode simply won't appear. But the corrective practice is still valuable: **finish the hip drive before you pull under.** The bar should be driven high by your hips, not by your arms. Your arms are just getting out of the way. When the second pull feels like your hips are the engine and your arms are passengers, the bar path is vertical. Practice that at 73 kg so the pattern is automatic at 79 kg next week.
>
> **No compensation sets:**
> If a rep is missed, note it in the table and move on. This is the habit to build starting now. One missed rep out of 6 on a deload is not a failure — chasing it with a 4th set at RPE 6+ on a recovery day is. The program has already demonstrated the cost of compensation sets (Week 3 set 6 grooved the failure pattern). Don't repeat it. Note it. Move on.
>
> **Squat-receive mandatory:**
> Even at 73 kg, even if you can power snatch it with room to spare, ride the bar to a full squat catch on every rep. The squat receive is a skill, and skills atrophy without practice. You rebuilt the squat clean receiving position on Saturday with 15 deliberate reps — do the same today with the snatch. Every rep: pull, turn over, descend to the bottom. Pause for 1 second if it helps you feel the catch position. Then stand.
>
> **Expected RPE:** 3–4 per rep. If any rep feels above RPE 5, something is mechanically off — check bar path or check your fatigue level (deload may have been insufficient if Wednesday or Thursday loaded hard).

> [!info]- Clean & Jerk — Mechanism & Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **What 90 kg is:**
> 90 kg is the split between clean 70% (98 kg) and jerk 70% (84 kg) — the phase file uses a compromise weight to train both in a single session. At 90 kg: the clean is 64% of your 140 kg PR, the jerk is 75% of your 120 kg PR. The clean should feel like a warm-up. The jerk is the slightly harder portion but still well within deload range.
>
> **— SQUAT CLEAN EVERY REP —**
>
> This is non-negotiable today. Not optional. Not "power clean is fine." Every rep is a squat clean.
>
> Here's why this matters specifically at deload weight: 90 kg is light enough that your body will default to a power clean automatically. You can easily power clean 90 kg — you've been doing 120 kg power cleans at the box all month. The deload weight means zero fatigue excuse, zero strength excuse. If you power clean it today, it's a choice, not a necessity. Make the right choice: pull from the floor, receive in a full front squat, sit in the bottom for 1 second, stand, reset, jerk.
>
> The Saturday squat clean session (15 reps at 85 kg) rebuilt the receiving position pattern. Today continues that repair. If you power clean instead, you're erasing it.
>
> **— ANTERIOR SHOULDER MONITORING: THE THIRD DATA POINT —**
>
> This is the most important diagnostic element of today's session. Here's the full picture going into today:
>
> | Session | Load | Anterior Shoulder Pull | Conditions |
> | --- | --- | --- | --- |
> | Week 3 Friday | 98 kg | PRESENT | Fatigued, day 5 of peak volume, decayed squat clean pattern |
> | Week 3 Saturday | 85 kg | NOT PRESENT | Fresh-ish, deliberate positional work, pause catches |
> | Week 4 Wednesday | 98 kg | Pending | Fresh, deload week, squat clean prescribed |
> | **Week 4 Friday (today)** | **90 kg** | **?** | **Fresh, 4 rest days since last heavy session** |
>
> **What to feel for:** A stretch or pull sensation in the anterior shoulder joint — the front of the shoulder capsule, approximately where the pec meets the front delt — that appears during the pull-under phase of the squat clean (when the bar is rising and you're pulling yourself underneath it). It was specifically absent on power cleans (even at 120 kg), which suggests the mechanism is in the pull-under, not the pull itself.
>
> **Possible explanations, ranked by current evidence:**
> 1. **Load + pattern decay:** The shoulder pull appeared at 98 kg after three weeks of power-clean-dominated box sessions. The motor pattern for the squat clean pull-under had decayed, causing the arms to reach forward (instead of directly up-and-under) to catch the bar. Saturday at 85 kg with deliberate technique: shoulder pull absent. This is the most likely explanation — the pattern was the issue, not structural.
> 2. **Load-dependent above ~88 kg:** If the shoulder pull appears today at 90 kg despite fresh conditions and good positioning, the threshold may be load-based regardless of technique. Above a certain weight, the anterior capsule is consistently stressed.
> 3. **Thoracic restriction cascade:** Your thoracic restriction (worse on left) limits how high the elbows can get in the front rack catch. Under heavy load and with suboptimal pull-under timing, the shoulder has to compensate for a low elbow catch — pulling forward instead of absorbing vertically. This is mechanically consistent and could create a load threshold.
>
> **What to do if it appears today:**
> Note it immediately in the daily log. If it appears on set 1, continue with sets 2–3 but focus on keeping the bar closer on the pull-under (bar path more vertical). Do not push past RPE 6 on the clean. After the session, the Week 5 pre-clean protocol gets added: **banded shoulder ER mobilization (2 x 30 sec each, external rotation against light band), sleeper stretch (1 x 45 sec each side), and 5 deliberate empty-bar squat clean pull-unders with a pause at the bottom** — all before loading the bar.
>
> **What to do if it does NOT appear today:**
> Log it as absent. Continue monitoring at 100+ kg in Week 5. The shoulder issue may be genuinely resolved by the Saturday positional work. But it's not closed until it stays absent at 98–105 kg in fresh conditions.
>
> **— JERK FROM SQUAT CLEAN —**
>
> Every jerk in today's session comes out of a squat clean. This matters for two reasons:
>
> **1. Thoracic engagement.** The squat clean receiving position — elbows driven forward and high, chest tall, eyes on the wall — creates better thoracic extension than receiving from a rack. Your body is already in a tall-chest position going into the jerk dip. Compare this to Tuesday's jerk from the rack, where you may start slightly less extended. The clean sets up the position; use it.
>
> **2. Knee data.** Week 2 data: jerk after squat clean (Friday) = zero knee pain. Jerk from the rack (Thursday, 86 kg) = 2–3/10 pain. The pattern held in Week 3 — jerk after squat clean at 95–98 kg was consistently 5/5 knee. This is one of the clearest athlete-specific data points in the program: **for you, the jerk is safer and better when it follows a squat clean.** Today's 90 kg should be pain-free.
>
> **Jerk dip mechanics:**
> Wider stance is the default now (confirmed since Week 3 Tuesday as better COM management). "Heels heavy through the dip" stays as the primary cue. Consistent depth — not shallow (lost drive) or too deep (COM forward). At 90 kg deload weight, the jerk should feel like a crisp drill, not a grind. RPE on the jerk: ≤5–6.
>
> **Clean stand-up before jerk:**
> After the squat clean, pause 1 second in the bottom, then stand fully. Reset your feet to jerk stance. Take a breath. Dip. The temptation at deload weight is to make it look like a fluid clean-to-jerk sequence — but that's a coordination skill for Phase 2 when the timing is automatic. Today, treat them as two separate lifts. Own the clean. Own the jerk.
>
> **Expected RPE:** Clean ≤4–5. Jerk ≤5–6. If either feels above RPE 6, something is off — check technique before adding a 4th set.

## Gymnastics Skill: Handstand Walk Progressions (5–10 min)

- [ ] Wall-facing handstand hold: 2 x 40 sec
- [ ] Wall walk with shoulder taps: 2 x 8 each hand
- [ ] Wall-facing HS lateral weight shifts: 2 x 5 each direction

> [!info]- Handstand Walk Progressions — Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Deload volume: 2 sets, not 3.**
> This is a maintain-only block. The skill progressions are identical to Week 3 Friday's prescription, but each drill drops from 3 sets to 2. The goal is to keep the neuromuscular pattern alive — not to accumulate fatigue. After a full deload week, your shoulder pressing capacity has recovered. Two quality sets are enough to hold the gains from Weeks 1–3.
>
> **No freestanding handstand attempts today.**
> You hit a 5-second freestanding hold in Week 2, which is ahead of schedule for Phase 1. That's a significant achievement, and it's not going anywhere. Freestanding attempts require central nervous system readiness and confidence — deload week is not the environment for pushing that limit. The wall work today is the foundation; the freestanding work resumes in Week 5 when energy is high.
>
> **Wall-facing handstand hold — 2 x 40 sec:**
> Hands ~6 inches from the wall, face toward the wall, full handstand. Body stacked: wrists under shoulders under hips under heels. Press down through the floor, spread the fingers, push the floor away. 40 seconds is the same hold from Week 3 — it should feel easier now. If 40 seconds feels very easy, move your hands 1–2 inches closer to the wall on the second set to increase the challenge without extending time.
>
> **Wall walk with shoulder taps — 2 x 8 each hand:**
> Start in a push-up position, walk your feet up the wall, walk your hands toward the wall until your chest touches (or as close as mobility allows). At the top of the wall walk, tap your right shoulder with your left hand, return hand to floor, then tap your left shoulder with your right hand — that's 2 taps per position. 8 taps each hand = 16 total taps per set. Move deliberately: tap, pause, return — don't rush. The shoulder tap at the top requires single-arm weight bearing in an inverted position. That's the lateral stability you're building toward handstand walking.
>
> **Lateral weight shifts — 2 x 5 each direction:**
> Wall-facing handstand position (hands ~6 inches from wall). Shift your weight from the right hand to the left hand — just enough to unweight the right hand slightly. Then shift back to center, then shift to load the left hand and unweight the right. 5 shifts per direction. The motion is subtle — you're not lifting a hand off the floor (though that can happen naturally). You're practicing lateral weight transfer in the inverted position. This is the most direct training for handstand walking of all three drills: walking is just continuous lateral weight shifts with a step between each one.
>
> **Post-Oly shoulder state:**
> The snatch and C&J today will have put some load through the shoulders (especially the snatch catch and jerk overhead). Watch for any residual anterior shoulder fatigue from the C&J — if it feels inflamed or sore heading into the HS work, skip the lateral shifts (2 sets of holds only) and note it in the log.

> [!info]- Accessory — Mechanism & Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Peterson Step-Downs — 2 x 10 each (maintain, don't increase)**
> This is the second knee-rehab dose of Week 4. Monday was the first. The asymmetric protocol is unchanged:
> - **Left leg:** 10–15 cm step, 8–10 kg KB held in front. Shorter step keeps the knee angle manageable for the patellar tracking issue. Focus on vertical torso and controlled eccentric (3 seconds down). The left VMO is the target — it's what's been identified as underactivated relative to the right.
> - **Right leg:** 20 cm step, 8–10 kg KB. Full ROM, controlled eccentric. The right can handle more depth because the patellar tracking is unaffected.
>
> The goal of Friday step-downs isn't to progress — it's to create the second weekly dose of VMO stimulus. Tendons adapt on a 24–48 hour stimulus-recovery cycle, and twice-per-week step-downs is the minimum effective frequency for patellar rehab. Don't skip these.
>
> **Left hip external rotation stiffness — monitor today:**
> Saturday's warm-up noted "stiff gear" sensation in left hip external rotation. This is common after peak-volume weeks and usually resolves with a full deload. Check: does the left hip ER feel smoother today than it did Saturday? If it's still rough, that's a Week 5 warm-up flag — add targeted ER mobilization (standing figure-4, sidestep band walks) before Monday's squat session.
>
> **Pigeon Stretch — 2 x 60 sec each side:**
> Deep external rotator (piriformis, obturator group) release. Get into pigeon position, hips square, fold forward. 60 seconds is long enough to get genuine tissue response — not just "stretch and move on." Breathe slowly. Left side first. This directly addresses the hip ER stiffness flagged Saturday and supports BSS depth next week.
>
> **90/90 Hip Rotation — 2 x 10 each side:**
> Seated on the floor, both legs at 90 degrees, rotate from one side to the other while keeping the torso tall. Two more reps per side than Week 2's prescription (which was 2 x 8). The difficulty is maintaining a tall torso during the rotation — resist the temptation to lean away from the movement. This rotation trains the capsular mobility that lets the hip move freely through the full ROM required in the squat clean catch. Do it at the end when the body is warm and the hip capsule has been opened by the pigeon stretch.
>
> **Full hip mobility circuit — 10 min total:**
> Between the pigeon stretch and the 90/90 hip rotation, spend any remaining time on additional hip work: 10 hip circles each leg, 30 sec banded hip flexor stretch each side, or a 90-second forward fold. The deload is the best time to pour time into mobility work that gets compressed during peak weeks.
