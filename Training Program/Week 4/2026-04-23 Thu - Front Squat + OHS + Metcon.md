> [!info]- Warm-up Movement Guide (click to expand)
>
> **90/90 Breathing — 2 min**
> This is not optional today and it's not just a warm-up filler. The 90/90 breathing protocol — done consistently every session for two weeks — is the primary reason the lumbar-pelvic constraint resolved last Thursday. Here's the mechanism: lying on your back with feet on the wall, you breathe into the posterior ribcage and allow the lumbar spine to flatten against the floor. This trains the diaphragm and deep abdominals to create an intra-abdominal pressure pattern that posteriorly tilts the pelvis — the opposite of your baseline anterior tilt. After two weeks, the pattern is becoming automatic under load. Do it every warm-up, every session, for the rest of the program. It takes 2 minutes and it's working.
>
> **Thoracic Foam Roll — 2 min (extended today)**
> Mid-back, bar perpendicular to the spine. Spend longer than usual — move slowly through each thoracic segment from T6 to T12, extending over the roller and breathing deeply at each position. With the front squat and OHS coming up, thoracic extension is the bottleneck for both movements: the front rack depends on it for elbow height, and the OHS depends on it for a vertical torso with the bar behind the ears.
>
> **Front Rack Stretch with Barbell — 60 sec**
> Bar in rack at shoulder height. Step in, place bar across the front delts, then aggressively push the elbows up and forward. This is your weekly baseline check on left-side thoracic restriction. Has the left elbow been getting higher over the past three weeks? Compare the feel today to your first impression in Week 1.
>
> **Ankle Dorsiflexion Wall Stretch — 2 x 30 sec each side (extra emphasis today)**
> Foot about 4 inches from the wall, push the knee over the big toe toward the wall without the heel lifting. If the knee touches the wall easily, step the foot back further. Limited ankle dorsiflexion forces the heel to rise in the squat — driving forward lean, forward bar path, and extra demand on the lumbar spine. This is why it's in every warm-up and getting extra time in the deload. Compare left vs right: note which side has less range.
>
> **Goblet Squat x 10**
> Light KB or empty bar held at the chest. Full depth, elbows inside knees at the bottom. Opens the hips, warms the quad and glute, and reinforces the upright torso without loading the spine. Notice if the lumbar-pelvic constraint is still absent — it should be at bodyweight.
>
> **OHS with Empty Bar x 5**
> Snatch-width grip, active press up (slight shrug), hook grip. Practice the eye gaze: pick a spot on the wall at eye level 2-3 m ahead and hold it through all 5 reps. Practice the "push through the bar" cue — this is your medial elbow pain prevention protocol from last week. The warm-up is where you lock in the habit.

>

(2) Wider stance adopted during jerk work (Week 3 Tue) and applied to squats — reduces adductor tension that was pulling the pelvis forward at depth. (3) Accumulated squat volume building positional familiarity at controlled loads.
>
> Today's practical implication: 104 kg with no tempo, after last week's 107 kg at 3-0-0 tempo, should feel genuinely light. The absence of the lumbar constraint means you can descend to full depth without hitting the old ceiling. Don't force it — squat to the depth that feels solid and controlled. But expect more range than you had three weeks ago.
>
> **What to monitor going forward:** The constraint resolved at Phase 1 loads (65-70% front squat). It may re-emerge as Phase 2 brings loads above 80%. If it does, the management tools are the same: wider stance, 90/90 breathing, controlled depth. But it should be significantly less pronounced given the progress so far.
>
> **Eye Gaze Protocol for Front Squat**
>
> This was identified as a recurring gap across Week 3 (raised in Coach's Reviews for Monday, Thursday, and again in Thursday's OHS). The protocol:
>
> - Pick a spot on the wall at eye level, approximately 2-3 meters ahead. Not the floor (causes elbows to drop), not the ceiling (causes neck hyperextension and bar drift forward).
> - Before your first rep in the warm-up, find the spot. A light switch, a seam in the wall, a mark on the rig — anything at eye level, fixed. Lock onto it.
> - Cue: "eyes on the wall, elbows UP." The two cues are linked — when the gaze drifts down, the elbows follow. When you hold the gaze at eye level, the elbows stay higher naturally.
> - Hold the same spot through all five reps of a set. Don't track the bar, don't look at the floor between reps. The breathing pause at the top is still looking at the wall.
>
> At 104 kg with no tempo, you have significantly more mental bandwidth than last week's 107 kg at 3-0-0. Use that bandwidth to practice and confirm the eye gaze habit. By the end of set 3, it should feel automatic.
>
> **Elbow Reset Habit at the Top**
>
> Last Thursday (sets 4-5 at 107 kg), the elbows dropped under sustained thoracic extensor fatigue, the bar shifted off the deltoid shelf, and the wrists caught the load. The fix is the breathing pause at the top: stand fully, drive the elbows up aggressively, verify the bar is on the shelf (not in the hands), then brace and descend.
>
> At 104 kg no-tempo today, thoracic fatigue shouldn't be a factor. But the habit of resetting elbows at the top needs to be deliberate and practiced at light loads so it becomes automatic at heavy loads. Before every rep: elbows UP, then down.
>
> **No tempo today — what that means**
>
> Week 3 used 3-0-0 tempo (3-second eccentric), which adds approximately 12-15% effective load. Today there's no tempo — descend in about 2 seconds at a controlled pace, no pause, drive out of the bottom. This is not slow, but it's not a crash dive either. The focus is on positions and smoothness, not time-under-tension. At 104 kg, the weight should feel almost easy. If it doesn't by set 2, something technical is off — check the elbow position and the breathing.
>
> **Expected RPE at 104 kg (no tempo):** ≤5, target ~4. If any set reaches RPE 6, stop and troubleshoot — check elbow height, check breathing, check if wider stance is being used. Do not push through an RPE 6+ set today.

> [!info]- Overhead Squat — Mechanism & Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Why OHS in the program**
> The overhead squat is the receiving position for the snatch. Training it as a standalone movement at sub-maximal loads builds the specific combination of overhead stability, thoracic extension, ankle dorsiflexion, and hip mobility required for a solid snatch catch. The snatch efficiency gap (55% snatch-to-squat ratio — 105/190) is primarily positional and technical. A stronger, more stable OHS position directly transfers to higher snatch catches.
>
> **Week 4 at 58 kg (vs 70 kg last week)**
> This is intentionally much lighter than Week 3's 70 kg (used because 8 kg plates weren't available). At 58 kg, the overhead position should feel stable and easy. The work today is entirely positional and habitual — not a strength stimulus. Three sets of five reps at this load. Focus on the cue sequence and the feel of a clean overhead position, not on completing reps.
>
> **The medial elbow pain protocol (from Week 3 Thu)**
> Week 3 set 1: tight closed-fist grip overhead created compressive force on the medial epicondyle. Resolved completely on sets 2-4 by switching to hook grip and actively pressing up through the bar. Mechanism: tight grip engages wrist flexors and forearm muscles, which load the medial epicondyle especially when the wrist is slightly extended overhead. Hook grip passively wraps the thumb, reducing forearm grip muscle activation. "Push through the bar" shifts stabilization demand to the triceps and shoulder extensors (larger muscles, no medial elbow involvement). This is now standard OHS protocol — always.
>
> **Cue sequence for every set:**
> 1. Hook grip before taking the bar — thumb tucked, fingers over.
> 2. Press up actively ("push through the bar") before descending — engage the shoulder complex, not the forearm grip.
> 3. "Knuckles to ceiling" — the hand cue that keeps the wrist in slight extension and the shoulder in external rotation. Pressing the knuckles up keeps the bar balanced over the mid-foot.
> 4. Eye gaze: wall at eye level, 2-3 m ahead — same as front squat. The OHS demands a near-vertical torso (bar behind the ears), so gaze is close to horizontal. Don't look up at the bar — this pulls the torso into hyperextension and causes forward bar drift.
>
> **Full depth**
> With the lumbar-pelvic constraint resolved, go to full depth. The OHS at 58 kg should allow complete depth with no lumbar stress. Notice if the ankle dorsiflexion warm-up made a difference — your heels should stay down throughout. If one heel wants to rise, compare left vs right: that's your ankle mobility asymmetry showing up.
>
> **Connection to snatch**
> Every OHS rep at any weight is snatch receive practice. The overhead position you build here — active shoulders, vertical torso, bar behind ears, stable hip crease below parallel — is the same position you're trying to catch 105-120 kg in. The carryover is direct. Notice what feels unstable or effortful at 58 kg: that's what needs work before heavier snatches in Phase 2.
>
> **Expected RPE at 58 kg:** ≤4. If it's RPE 5+, something technical is off — likely the hook grip wasn't applied, the shoulder isn't fully engaged, or the thoracic warm-up wasn't sufficient. Stop, reset, and retry.

> [!info]- Mobility Circuit — Mechanism & Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Why this is the main event today**
>
> In a deload week, the strength work (3x5 at 65%) is a maintenance stimulus, not a developmental one. The mobility circuit is where developmental work happens. Thoracic restriction, ankle dorsiflexion, overhead shoulder mobility, and hip ER stiffness are the primary physical ceilings on this program's Oly lifting goals — not strength. Fifteen deliberate minutes here compounds differently than a 15-minute set of accessories at the end of a hard session. Give each piece real time and attention.
>
> ---
>
> **Thoracic Extension Over Bench with Barbell — 3 x 10 breaths**
>
> *Target limiter: Thoracic mobility restriction (bilateral, worse on left) — directly caps front rack elbow height and jerk dip mechanics.*
>
> Setup: Kneel in front of a bench. Place elbows on the bench with hands in a prayer position (clasped together). Sit the hips back toward the heels, allowing the chest to drop toward the floor. The bar can be placed across the elbows/forearms for added load (gravity). Breathe deeply — each inhale expands the ribcage, each exhale allows the chest to sink further. 10 slow breaths per set.
>
> This is more effective than the foam roller version because the load (gravity + elbows fixed on the bench) provides a sustained end-range stretch that improves thoracic extension ROM over time. The front rack elbow height is directly gated by thoracic extension — as this improves, the elbow height on both front squats and jerks will increase without any deliberate elbow cueing. This is where that adaptation is built.
>
> Rest 30 seconds between sets. Compare how deep the chest sinks on set 3 vs set 1.
>
> ---
>
> **Ankle Dorsiflexion Stretch (Wall) — 2 x 30 sec each side**
>
> *Target limiter: Ankle dorsiflexion deficit — contributes to forward lean in squats, limits depth, increases lumbar demand.*
>
> Same as the warm-up version but held longer. Foot 4-5 inches from the wall, push the knee directly over the big toe, heel stays flat. If the knee touches the wall easily, move the foot back until you feel a real stretch in the Achilles and posterior lower leg. The goal is range — can you touch the wall with the knee further from the wall each week?
>
> Why this matters: Limited ankle dorsiflexion means the tibia can't travel forward enough during the squat descent. The body compensates by leaning the torso forward (adding lumbar load) or by rising onto the toes (destabilizing the base). Both compensations cap front squat and OHS performance at heavier loads. Two sets of 30 seconds, twice a week, does accumulate real change — but only if you're at true end-range and not half-stretching.
>
> Note which side has less range. For most right-dominant athletes, left ankle dorsiflexion is marginally more limited. Match the weaker side's depth on the stronger side.
>
> ---
>
> **Banded Overhead Distraction — 2 x 30 sec each arm (extra time on left)**
>
> *Target limiter: Thoracic mobility restriction + shoulder capsule restriction — limits OHS stability and snatch receive position, worse on left.*
>
> Attach a band to the rig at shoulder height or above. Grip the band with one hand (standing grip, thumb up). Step away from the rig until the band is taut and pulling your arm forward and slightly up. Allow the arm to be pulled into end-range flexion/elevation while keeping the shoulder blade back and the ribcage down (don't arch the lower back to compensate). Breathe and hold for 30 seconds.
>
> The distraction creates space in the shoulder joint capsule (the band pulls the humeral head slightly out of the socket), allowing passive end-range stretching that cannot be achieved by active pressing. This is especially effective for the left side — left arm is more rigid in the front rack and receives the snatch less cleanly than the right.
>
> Spend a full 30 seconds on the right arm, then 30-40 seconds on the left (extra time on the more restricted side). Notice if the OHS with empty bar in the warm-up felt any different after doing this last week.
>
> ---
>
> **Pigeon Stretch — 2 x 60 sec each side (left hip emphasis)**
>
> *Target limiter: Left hip ER (external rotation) stiffness — identified Saturday (April 18) as a new finding during squat clean unilateral work.*
>
> Standard pigeon: front shin roughly parallel to the front of the mat, back leg extended behind. Allow the hip to sink toward the floor. For a deeper stretch, fold the torso forward over the front leg. The hip external rotators (piriformis, gemelli, obturator group) are the primary target — these are the muscles that allow the hip to open at the bottom of a squat and in the split jerk landing.
>
> Left hip ER stiffness shows up as roughness in the deep squat position and in the split stance (left foot forward). If the left hip was tighter than the right on Saturday, this is the targeted intervention. Sixty seconds is the minimum effective dose for a sustained capsular/muscular stretch — shorter holds don't produce lasting change.
>
> Hold left side first, then right. Compare: does the left hip drop to the floor as easily as the right? Is there any gripping or discomfort in the piriformis or outer glute? Note this in the daily log — tracking whether left hip ER roughness is improving or static is useful data for the Phase 2 squat loading.
>
> ---
>
> **90/90 Hip Rotation — 2 x 10 each side**
>
> *Target: Hip mobility across the full rotation range — complements pigeon stretch for ER and adds IR (internal rotation) work.*
>
> Sit on the floor with both legs in a 90/90 position (front leg externally rotated, back leg internally rotated, both knees at roughly 90 degrees). Rotate the pelvis so the back knee tips toward the floor, then rotate back to the starting position. 10 controlled rotations each side.
>
> This addresses both directions of hip rotation — the pigeon targets ER (external rotation), the 90/90 rotation adds IR (internal rotation) to the circuit. Hip IR is often more limited and more neglected. A full rotation circuit (ER + IR) balances hip mobility and reduces the lateral hip stiffness that shows up as subtle knee tracking deviation — directly relevant to the left knee patellar tracking issue. With the lumbar-pelvic constraint now resolved, hip rotation work is the next layer of movement quality to improve.

> - At a **clean-width grip** (hands roughly shoulder-width, elbows wide), the elbows have to drive forward and slightly out to keep the bar on the deltoid shelf. Driving the elbows out forces shoulder external rotation, which actively retracts and depresses the scapulae and locks the thoracic spine into extension. **A locked-extended thoracic spine is geometrically incapable of rounding under moderate load.** The "less tendency to round" you felt isn't willpower — it's that the position physically prevents it. The cost: the wider elbow angle is mechanically less stable for the bar (smaller rack contact area, more wrist demand), so it *feels* less strong even though the back position is better.
>
> - At a **narrow grip** (hands closer in, elbows tighter), the elbows can drive straight up rather than out. This sits the bar more securely on the shelf, the wrists are more neutral, and the load path through the arms feels more efficient — hence "feels stronger." But less elbow-out demand means less external rotation, less scapular retraction, and less mandatory thoracic extension. The thoracic spine is now *allowed* to flex if anything else (fatigue, weak braces, ankle limit, COM drift) pushes it that way. As soon as the bar gets even slightly forward of mid-foot — which is exactly what you describe — the upper back is the path of least resistance, and it rounds.
>
> So the trade-off is: **clean grip = better position, slightly worse rack security. Narrow grip = better rack security, position must be defended actively rather than passively.** For Phase 1, where the entire goal is positional foundation, **clean-width is the right grip for you specifically** — it's a passive defense against the exact failure mode (forward COM, back round) that you've identified as your tendency at heavier loads. At Phase 2 loads (130+ kg) the case gets even stronger, because that's where rounding under fatigue starts costing reps.
>
> One caveat: clean-width grip puts more demand on thoracic extension and front rack mobility — both of which are on your active limiter list. The fact that you can hold clean-width comfortably at 104 kg now (which would have been hard in Week 1) is real evidence that the mobility work is paying off. **Default to clean-width going forward**, and let the narrow grip be a tool for genuinely heavy near-max sets in Phase 3 if you ever choose it.
