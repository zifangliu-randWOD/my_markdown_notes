# Tuesday — Week 4: Light Jerk + Strict Press + Ring MU (Deload)

[[00 - Program Overview]] | [[Phase 1 - Weeks 1-4]]

> [!tip] Day Focus
> This is the most important jerk session of Phase 1 — not because the weight is heavy, but because what you cement here at 84 kg becomes the mechanical baseline you carry into Weeks 5-8 and beyond. The wider stance (+5 cm outside hip-width) is no longer experimental: it is your **permanent default jerk stance** from today forward. At 70% load, every cognitive rep goes to proprioception and motor patterning, not survival. Feel the heels staying heavy through the dip. Feel the knee staying stacked over the foot rather than diving forward. Feel the difference a 5 cm shift makes in COM control. The deload week exists precisely to create this kind of clean slate — lower load, lower fatigue, maximum attention to what matters. By the end of today's three sets, wider stance should feel like home.

> [!info]- Warm-up Movement Guide (click to expand)
>
> **90/90 Breathing — 2 min**
> Reset before you do anything else. Floor, feet on wall, press your lower back flat with exhale. Three to four complete breath cycles. Lumbar-pelvic position has been stable for several weeks — keep that streak going by not skipping this.
>
> **Banded Lat Stretch x 45 sec each side**
> Band at head height, grip and step away, lean into the stretch. Left side first, then right, extra time on left. Tight lats externally rotate the humerus and close down the front rack — opening them is a direct prerequisite to the thoracic engagement cue you need before each jerk set.
>
> **Banded Front Rack Stretch x 60 sec each side**
> This is doing double duty today — it serves as both warm-up and early accessory work. Band at elbow height, push elbows into the deepest front rack you can tolerate. Left elbow is more restricted; spend extra time there. Focus on the sensation of the ribcage lifting and the thoracic spine extending. This is the exact thoracic position you need to own before the jerk dip.
>
> **Thoracic Rotation on Wall x 8 each side**
> Inside arm flat on wall, rotate away. Find the stiff spot and breathe into it. Week 3 established that thoracic engagement is worse when jerking from the rack than from a clean (the clean sets the position automatically). This drill primes the thoracic extensors so you can recreate that position intentionally from the rack.
>
> **Dip Calibration Drill — foam roller behind heels**
> This drill was introduced in Week 3 and is critical today. Place a foam roller (or a low box) behind your heels at a height where your glutes just lightly graze it at the correct dip depth — approximately 10-15 cm of knee bend. Do 5 empty-bar push jerks touching the target each time. Then remove the roller and do 3 more reps, replicating the same depth from proprioception alone. The goal is to rebuild the tactile reference after a week of fatigue. At 84 kg, there is no reason to hunt for dip depth — this drill makes it automatic.
>
> **Wider stance from rep 1 on the empty bar**
> Do not start narrow and widen later. Start every warm-up rep with the wider stance — approximately 5 cm outside hip-width. The motor pattern you train in the warm-up is the motor pattern that shows up under load. Own the new default from the first rep of the day.
>
> **Jerk Footwork Drills x 10**
> Dip and drive feet into the push jerk landing position (feet simultaneously move to shoulder-width split or balanced landing). Practice from the wider dip base — the drive foot positions shift slightly when the dip base is wider. Fast feet, controlled catch.

## Primary: Push Jerk from Rack

3 x 3 @ 70% — 84 kg
- **Default stance: wider (approximately +5 cm each foot outside hip-width) — this is not optional or experimental, this is the stance**
- Cue 1: "heels heavy through the dip" — whole foot pressure, not toe-dominant
- Cue 2: "elbows up, chest up, big breath" — thoracic set before every single rep from the rack
- All 3 sets should feel fast, crisp, and well-controlled. RPE cap: ≤6
- If left knee is still uncomfortable at 3.5/5 despite wider stance: reduce to 75 kg for remaining sets and note the mechanism — this would indicate a different aggravator than COM drift

| Set | Weight | Reps | RPE | Stance | Notes |
| --- | ------ | ---- | --- | ------ | ----- |
| 1   | 84 kg  | 3/3  | 5.5 | Wide   |       |
| 2   | 84 kg  | 3/3  | 6   | Wide   |       |
| 3   | 84 kg  | 3/3  | 6   | Wide   |       |

> [!info]- Push Jerk — Mechanism & Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Why 84 kg and why 3 sets of 3?**
> Week 3 Tuesday was 90 kg x 6x3 — the peak overhead volume session of Phase 1. Today at 84 kg x 3x3 is a 40% volume reduction and a 6 kg load drop. The purpose is not to build fitness. The purpose is to ingrain the wider stance as an automatic default under load, while accumulated fatigue clears and the nervous system recovers. At 84 kg (70%), the bar is light enough that you can focus entirely on WHERE your feet are, WHERE the pressure is through your foot, and HOW the knee tracks — rather than splitting attention between those cues and surviving a heavy rep. This is peak learning efficiency.
>
> **Why wider stance is now the permanent default — the full mechanical case:**
> Week 3 Tuesday established the finding empirically: a stance approximately 5 cm wider than hip-width per foot naturally keeps COM over the heel during the dip. Here is why it works mechanically:
> - A wider base shortens the effective depth of each centimeter of knee travel — you reach the same dip depth with less forward knee excursion.
> - Less forward knee excursion means less anterior shear force on the patella — this is the exact pain mechanism in your left knee (patellar tracking aggravated by forward COM/knee-forward positions under load).
> - A wider stance also shifts the moment arm of the quads so they drive more vertically than anteriorly — which reinforces the heel-loaded dip rather than working against it.
> - The wider stance was simultaneously helping with COM drift AND knee pain, which suggests they were the same problem: forward COM loading the anterior knee. Fixing one fixed both.
>
> This is not a temporary workaround for a sore knee. This is a superior jerk dip mechanics position for your anatomy. The deload week is the ideal time to cement it because the low load removes all barriers to doing it exactly right.
>
> **The "heels heavy" cue — mechanics breakdown:**
> Imagine your foot as a triangle with three pressure points: heel, base of big toe, base of little toe. A correct dip maintains equal pressure across all three. What you feel as "forward COM drift" is the pressure triangle collapsing — heel lifts, pressure pools at the balls of the feet and toes. When that happens, the knee tracks forward to compensate, the bar path tilts forward, and the anterior knee loads. "Heels heavy" is shorthand for: keep that triangle complete. The test is simple — at the bottom of the dip, can you wiggle your toes? If yes, the heel has pressure and the foot is loaded correctly.
>
> **Thoracic set before every rep from the rack — why this matters:**
> Week 3 established that the jerk from a squat clean had zero knee pain at 95 kg (Week 2 Fri C&J), while the rack jerk at 90 kg had 3.5/5 knee pain. The leading hypothesis: the squat clean automatically loads the thoracic extensors through the catch, giving a pre-set thoracic position for the jerk. From the rack, that pre-set is absent unless you create it deliberately. The protocol: before unracking and before EVERY rep — (1) elbows UP so they're parallel with the floor or above, (2) chest UP by extending the thoracic spine, (3) big breath that visibly lifts the ribcage, THEN dip. Don't rush this. This is the front rack version of "bracing before you squat" — the sequence matters.
>
> **What "fast and crisp" means at 84 kg:**
> This weight should move. The dip should be controlled and shallow (use your calibration drill depth), the drive should be explosive through full extension of the hips and ankles, and the bar should lock overhead in a single smooth motion. If the bar is grinding up or the lockout is slow, something in the setup is off — usually the thoracic position or the dip tempo. At 70%, a slow rep is a technical fault, not a strength issue.
>
> **RPE target: ≤6 on all sets.**
> This should feel moderate at most. If any set is approaching RPE 7, it means the mechanics are working harder than the muscles — investigate which cue you're dropping.
>
> **The key question for today: does the knee stay at 5/5?**
> Tuesday is historically the only day in the week where left knee drops below 5/5 — Week 3 Tue was 3.5/5. With the wider stance locked in, the hypothesis is that the mechanism (forward COM → anterior knee loading) is resolved. Today is the test. If knee is 5/5 after all three sets, the fix works and we proceed with confidence into Week 5 loading. If it's still 3.5/5 despite wider stance and "heels heavy" cue, the aggravator is something else and we need to investigate (possibly thoracic collapse causing bar to drift forward and pull you onto your toes, or possible quad fatigue compensation from the week).

**Athlete Notes:**

## Secondary: Strict Press

3 x 8 @ ~50 kg
- Higher reps, lighter load — this is shoulder maintenance during deload, not a strength stimulus
- Dead stop to clavicle between every rep
- No leg drive — pure press. Bar stays over mid-foot (overhead, not in front)
- RPE cap: ≤6 — if this is harder than RPE 6, the weight is too heavy; strip to 47 kg

| Set | Weight | Reps | RPE | Notes |
| --- | ------ | ---- | --- | ----- |
| 1   | 50 kg  | 8/8  | 6   |       |
| 2   | 50 kg  | 8/8  | 7.5 |       |
| 3   | 50 kg  | 8/8  | 6   |       |

> [!info]- Strict Press — Mechanism & Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Why strict press today instead of push press?**
> Week 3 used push press (65-70 kg) as an overload stimulus — heavier loads overhead with leg assistance. That was a strength/overload stimulus during peak volume week. Deload reverses the logic: lighter weight, higher reps, no leg complexity. Strict press at 50 kg for 3x8 keeps the shoulder girdle active and maintains the pressing motor pattern without adding CNS load. Think of this as maintenance, not training.
>
> **Why 50 kg for 3x8 instead of 52 kg for 4x6?**
> Week 3 recalibrated strict press at 52 kg for 4x6 (RPE ~7). Deload cuts load slightly to 50 kg and shifts reps higher (8 vs 6) — same time under tension, less absolute load, fewer total sets (3 vs 4). This 40% volume reduction matches the deload protocol across all lifts.
>
> **The bar-rolling-in-palm issue from Week 3:**
> Week 3 noted the bar rolling toward the palm during push press, caused by high elbow position in the front rack. At 50 kg strict press, this issue is much less likely because: (1) the load is lower, so the drive phase impulse is gentler, and (2) strict press doesn't require the same aggressive dip that creates the elbows-high trade-off. Still — grip the bar firmly before each rep. Don't let it sit loosely in the hand. A firm grip from the start prevents the roll before it begins.
>
> **Execution checklist:**
> - Bar resting on upper chest/clavicle, clean grip (same as jerk)
> - Elbows slightly in front of the bar (not flared wide), forearms roughly vertical
> - Big breath and brace, then press — think of driving the head through between the arms as the bar passes the forehead, not after
> - Full overhead lockout — arms straight, biceps by or behind ears
> - Lower under control to clavicle, complete dead stop, re-brace, press again
> - No leaning back to make the rep easier — if you're leaning, the weight is too heavy
>
> **Athlete-specific — left shoulder:**
> Week 3 noted right-side dominance during both jerk and push press. At 50 kg strict press, this may show up as the left arm lagging or rotating slightly. Consciously drive both hands up at identical rates. If the bar tilts right, the left arm is behind — squeeze the left tricep harder at the sticking point.
>
> **Expected RPE at 50 kg:** RPE 4-5 for sets 1-2, possibly touching 5-6 by set 3 as the reps accumulate. If any set hits RPE 7+, reduce weight immediately. This is a deload.

**Athlete Notes:**
50 kg for 8 reps felt harder than expected. Dropped to 46 kg on the last set for 8 reps.

## Gymnastics Skill: Ring Muscle-Up Progressions (5-8 min)

2 sets of each — quality only, no maximal effort. Stop each set 1-2 reps before failure.

- [x] Deep ring dips: 2 x 8 (3-second negative, controlled) ✅ 2026-04-21
- [x] False grip pull-ups: 2 x 3 (unassisted if possible; band assist if needed — maintain false grip throughout) ✅ 2026-04-21
- [x] False grip ring rows to sternum: 2 x 5 (pull to sternum, not chin — controlled) ✅ 2026-04-21

> [!info]- Ring MU Progressions — Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Why only 2 sets during deload?**
> Week 3 ran 3 sets of each. The deload cuts volume by roughly 40% across the board — 2 sets represents that reduction while keeping the movement pattern alive. Gymnastics skills are neurological and technical rather than purely strength-based; a short but high-quality session maintains the motor pattern better than a rest week would. Two sets of focused, deliberate reps beats three rushed sets.
>
> **Deep ring dips: 2 x 8, 3-second negative**
> Lower yourself for a count of three seconds. At the bottom, elbows are fully bent and the rings are outside your armpits — this mimics the depth of the muscle-up transition. Then press out of the bottom under control. The slow negative builds the eccentric strength and positional awareness that makes the transition catch manageable. If 8 reps with a 3-second negative is too fatiguing by set 2, reduce to 6 reps — quality of each rep is the goal, not the rep count.
>
> **False grip pull-ups: 2 x 3**
> Hang from the rings in false grip — wrist over the top of the ring so the ring sits in the meat of the heel of the hand. Pull your chin above the rings, maintaining false grip throughout. If the grip slips under pulling load, use a resistance band for assistance but continue working the false grip position. Week 3 introduced this and by now the grip should be adapting — note whether it feels more stable than the first time. Three reps is deliberately conservative — the false grip transition from hang to support is where muscle-ups are won or lost, and grip integrity matters more than rep count.
>
> **False grip ring rows to sternum: 2 x 5**
> Feet on floor, lean back to roughly 45 degrees, pull your sternum to the rings. The key distinction from a chin-up: you are pulling HIGH — sternum to rings, not just chin over rings. This trains the pulling path of the actual muscle-up transition. The arms pull the rings to the hips, not just the hands to the chin. Maintain false grip throughout. If the sternum-to-rings path is impossible at 45 degrees, start more upright and gradually increase the lean as strength builds.
>
> **The deload purpose for skill work:**
> Reduced volume + lower fatigue = clearer proprioception. Use these two sets to feel the details: where exactly is the false grip pressure sitting in your hand? At the bottom of the ring dip, can you feel both rings equally or is one side drifting? During the row, can you keep the pulling path straight or does it arc? These observations inform Week 5 progression decisions.

## Accessory Window (15 min — extended mobility, deload focus)

- [x] Banded front rack stretch: 2 x 60 sec each side (extra time on left — go 70-75 sec if comfortable) ✅ 2026-04-21
- [x] Banded lat stretch: 2 x 45 sec each side ✅ 2026-04-21
- [x] Prone Y-T-W raises: 2 x 10 each position (bodyweight or very light plates) ✅ 2026-04-21
- [x] External rotation with band: 2 x 15 each arm ✅ 2026-04-21

> [!info]- Accessory Mobility — Mechanism & Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Why extended mobility today?**
> Deload week shifts the training emphasis from mechanical loading to structural recovery and tissue adaptation. The accessory window today is less about accessory strength and more about creating the thoracic and shoulder mobility changes that will allow better jerk mechanics, cleaner front rack, and ultimately a higher jerk ceiling. This 15 minutes of deliberate mobility work may produce more carry-over to Week 5+ performance than another accessory strength exercise would.
>
> **Banded front rack stretch: 2 x 60 sec each side**
> Band at approximately elbow height, grip and step away from the anchor so the band pulls the elbows into a deeper front rack. Push the elbow forward and up, not just forward. As the elbow travels into the front rack, feel the thoracic spine extending — this is the thoracic engagement you need before each jerk rep. Extra time on the left: the left arm is more restricted (thoracic restriction is bilateral but worse on the left per your profile). If 60 seconds feels productive on the left, extend to 70-75 seconds. This is the most important mobility drill in your program given the thoracic restriction's direct impact on jerk mechanics and clean receiving position.
>
> **Banded lat stretch: 2 x 45 sec each side**
> Band overhead at head height, grip and step away, lean into the stretch with the arm reaching overhead. Lats that are too tight pull the humerus into internal rotation and prevent the elbow from coming up in the front rack — the same mechanism as the thoracic restriction. Left side has been tighter historically. Breathe out as you relax deeper into the stretch. Don't hold your breath.
>
> **Prone Y-T-W raises: 2 x 10 each position**
> Lie face down, perform Y raises (arms forward-diagonal overhead), T raises (arms straight out to sides), and W raises (elbows bent 90 degrees, thumbs up). Bodyweight or very light plates (1-2 kg each hand). These target the lower and mid trapezius and rhomboids — the muscles responsible for scapular upward rotation and overhead stability. Without these, the jerk lockout relies too heavily on the deltoids and biceps, which fatigue faster and allow the bar to drift forward. Slow, controlled. No momentum.
>
> **External rotation with band: 2 x 15 each arm**
> Elbow at 90 degrees, upper arm against the side, rotate forearm outward against band resistance. Return slowly — don't let the band snap back. This targets the infraspinatus and teres minor (rotator cuff external rotators), which are chronically underactivated in athletes with anterior dominance (heavy bench, push emphasis). Two sets at 15 reps is maintenance volume — sufficient to keep the cuff activation pattern alive without adding fatigue during deload.
