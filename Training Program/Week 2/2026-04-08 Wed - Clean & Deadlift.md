## Warm-up (10 min)

- [ ] Hip circles (10 each direction, each leg)
- [ ] Banded good mornings x 15
- [ ] Clean grip deadlift to knee x 5
- [ ] Hang muscle clean x 5
- [ ] Front squat with empty bar x 5

> [!info]- Warm-up Movement Guide (click to expand)
>
> **Hip Circles (10 each direction, each leg)**
> General hip prep for the deep clean receiving position. Stand on one leg, draw large circles with the other knee — 10 forward, 10 backward, each side. This opens up the hip capsule in all directions and primes the joint for the deep squat catch position you'll need today. Don't rush — bigger, controlled circles are better than fast sloppy ones.
>
> **Banded Good Mornings x 15**
> Loop a band under your feet and behind your neck. Hinge at the hips, pushing them back, until you feel a strong hamstring stretch, then drive back up by squeezing your glutes. This activates the posterior chain (hamstrings, glutes, spinal erectors) — the primary movers for the clean pull and the deadlift. Keep your back flat throughout; if it rounds, the band tension is too high.
>
> **Clean Grip Deadlift to Knee x 5**
> Take a clean-width grip on an empty bar. Pull from the floor to just above the knee, then lower back down. This rehearses the first pull position — same back angle, same grip width, same weight distribution through the foot that you'll use when loaded. Focus: shins clear the bar by pushing the knees back, shoulders stay over the bar, back angle doesn't change. This is a position check, not a strength exercise.
>
> **Hang Muscle Clean x 5**
> From the hang position (bar at mid-thigh), extend the hips and pull the bar up, then whip the elbows through to catch it on the shoulders — no squat. This grooves the turnover and elbow speed without having to worry about catching deep. The muscle clean isolates the third pull (getting the elbows through) and directly tests your front rack mobility. If your left elbow is noticeably lower than your right on the catch, that's the thoracic restriction showing up — note it but don't force it.
>
> **Front Squat with Empty Bar x 5**
> Full depth front squat with a clean grip. This primes the receiving position for squat cleans and checks how your front rack feels today. Elbows high, torso vertical, full depth. If the left elbow drops or you feel restriction in the left wrist/lat, spend an extra 30 seconds in the bottom position with elbows driven up. This is your daily front rack status check.

> [!info]- Power Clean + Squat Clean Complex — Mechanism & Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Why this complex?**
> The power clean forces you to pull high and finish the extension. If you can't power clean it, your pull isn't complete — you're cutting the extension short and trying to get under too early. The squat clean that follows practices receiving deep. Doing both back to back teaches you the difference between pulling high (power) and pulling under (squat). This is the exact same principle as yesterday's snatch complex but applied to the clean — building positional awareness at submaximal load.
>
> **Week 2 context:**
> Weight goes from 98 kg (Week 1 planned) to 101 kg — a 3% jump. Last Wednesday's box session had you doing hang power clean + power clean up to 110 kg at RPE 8+. Those were heavier and from the hang (harder), so 101 kg from the floor should feel very manageable — RPE 5-6. The extra 3 kg over the planned Week 1 weight is a volume-building step, not an intensity challenge.
>
> **Connection to your limiters:**
> The front rack catch position directly tests your thoracic mobility. The left arm will be the indicator — does the left elbow sit lower than the right? Does the bar shift left or feel uneven on your shoulders? At 101 kg (72%), focus entirely on elbow speed in the turnover. If your elbows are slow, the bar crashes onto you and the catch feels heavy — that's not a strength problem, it's a turnover speed problem.
>
> **Execution — Power Clean (rep 1):**
> - Set your clean grip (just outside the shoulders), hook grip
> - First pull (floor to knee): slow and controlled. Push the floor away with your legs. Shoulders stay over the bar, back angle constant, shins clear the bar
> - Second pull (knee to hip): accelerate. Sweep the bar into your hip crease. Extend hard — jump the bar
> - Third pull (turnover): as the bar reaches max height, pull your elbows through fast — whip them around and up. Catch in a quarter-squat or power position
> - The bar should land cleanly on your front rack with minimal star-fishing
>
> **Execution — Squat Clean (rep 2):**
> - Reset at the floor, same setup
> - Same first and second pull as the power clean
> - Instead of catching in a quarter squat, pull yourself *under* the bar into a full front squat receiving position
> - Think "fast elbows and fast feet" as you drop under
> - Catch in a full depth front squat, pause for a beat to confirm stability, then stand
>
> **At 101 kg (72%):**
> RPE 5-6. The power clean should be comfortable, the squat clean even more so. If the catch feels heavy or jarring, the issue is likely elbow speed in the turnover, not pull strength. Film from the side if you can — watch for whether the bar loops forward during the turnover.

> [!info]- Clean-Grip Deadlift (Tempo) — Mechanism & Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Why clean-grip deadlifts?**
> This strengthens the exact pulling position used in cleans. The clean grip (narrower than conventional) forces more quad engagement and a more upright pulling position compared to a conventional deadlift. You're not training "deadlift strength" generically — you're training the specific back angle, grip width, and pulling mechanics that directly transfer to your clean.
>
> **Why tempo (3 up, 3 down)?**
> Tempo builds positional awareness — you can't rush through weak points. If your back rounds at a certain height, you'll feel it during a 3-second pull. If you shift to your toes or let the bar drift forward, 3 seconds of exposure makes it obvious. The 3-second descent teaches you to maintain your back angle on the way down — eccentric loading for tendon adaptation.
>
> **Week 2 adjustment context:**
> Last Wednesday's box session included 2x5 @ 140 kg conventional deadlifts (RPE 8) plus below-knee pause deadlifts at 130-140 kg. Those were non-tempo and from a conventional stance. Today's clean-grip tempo deadlifts are a different stimulus: narrower grip, slower pace, more positional demand. The effective load at 154 kg with a 3-0-3 tempo will feel significantly heavier than the number suggests — each set of 5 takes about 30 seconds of continuous tension.
>
> **Critical: Watch the tempo tax.**
> Week 1 repeatedly showed that tempo adds ~10-15% to effective load across all lifts. 154 kg with tempo may feel like 170-177 kg in perceived effort. If set 1 is RPE 7+ or set 2 is RPE 8+, drop to 140 kg. Straps are fine for tempo deadlifts if grip becomes the limiter — the point is positional training, not grip endurance.
>
> **Execution tips:**
> - Clean-width grip (hook grip or straps), feet hip-width, toes slightly out, shins touching the bar
> - 3-second pull: initiate by pushing the floor away. Count "one-thousand, two-thousand, three-thousand" from floor to lockout. Consistent speed — don't accelerate at the top
> - Lockout: hips fully extended, shoulders back, knees straight
> - 3-second descent: reverse the pull. Hips push back first, bar travels down your thighs, past your knees to the floor. Same 3-second count. Maintain your back angle — don't just drop it
> - Reset briefly at the floor (touch and go is fine as long as you don't bounce)
>
> **Connection to your limiters:**
> - For the left knee: clean-grip deadlift is relatively knee-friendly since it's more hip-dominant than squatting. The start position loads the knee (knees bent, shins angled), but the movement is primarily a hip hinge. This should not aggravate patellar tracking.
> - For pulling mechanics: this builds the patience and positional discipline needed off the floor in cleans. If you rush the first pull in a clean, you'll do the same here — but 3 seconds makes rushing impossible.

## Accessory Window (15-20 min)

- [ ] Bulgarian split squat: 3 x 10 each leg (DB — 12-15 kg each hand)
- [ ] Single-leg RDL: 3 x 10 each leg
- [ ] Banded hip flexor stretch: 2 x 45 sec each side

> [!info]- Accessory — Mechanism & Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Bulgarian Split Squat: 3 x 10 each leg — MANDATORY**
> This was missed in Week 1 (box session). You cannot skip this again — the left knee asymmetry correction depends on consistent unilateral quad work. Each leg works independently so you'll immediately feel which side is weaker.
>
> Week 2 progression: 3x8 → 3x10 reps, and use dumbbells (12-15 kg each hand) for load. Left leg first, match reps on right.
>
> *How to do it:* Stand about 2 feet in front of a bench, facing away. Place the top of your rear foot on the bench. Hold a dumbbell in each hand at your sides. Descend by bending your front knee until your back knee nearly touches the floor. Front knee tracks over toes — don't let it cave inward. Drive back up through your front heel.
>
> For the left knee: slight toe-out on the front foot (15-20 degrees) reduces patellar stress. If you feel patellar pain, check foot position first. Depth should be challenging but pain-free — if pain appears at full depth, stop 2 inches higher.
>
> **Single-Leg RDL: 3 x 10 each leg**
> Up from 3x8 in Week 1. Trains hamstring and glute unilaterally, plus balance and hip stability. This exposes hip stability differences between left and right and strengthens the posterior chain unilaterally.
>
> *How to do it:* Stand on one leg, hold a dumbbell in the opposite hand (contralateral loading). Hinge forward at the hip, keeping your back flat, reaching the dumbbell toward the floor while your free leg extends behind you. You should feel a deep hamstring stretch on the standing leg. Drive hips forward to stand. Start with 10-15 kg. Left leg first, match reps on right.
>
> If balance is a major limiter, lightly touch a wall or rack with your free hand — but only for balance, not support.
>
> **Banded Hip Flexor Stretch: 2 x 45 sec each side**
> Tight hip flexors pull your pelvis into anterior tilt, limiting your ability to stay upright in the squat catch position. Attach a heavy band to a low rig point behind you. Step into the band so it sits in your hip crease. Step forward into a half-kneeling position. The band pulls your hip forward and down, creating traction. Squeeze the glute on the kneeling side and lean slightly forward until you feel a deep stretch. Hold and breathe.
