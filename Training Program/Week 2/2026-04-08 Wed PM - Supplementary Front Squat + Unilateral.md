> [!info]- Front Squat — Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Why 3x3 at 100 kg?**
> The dropped Thursday session had 4x6 @ 104 kg with tempo. This is a reduced version — fewer reps, slightly less weight, no tempo — that preserves the front squat stimulus without overloading after this morning's pulling session. 100 kg at 62% should feel like a warm-up for your legs but a working set for your front rack position.
>
> **Execution:**
> - Clean the bar or take it from the rack. Elbows as high as possible — "elbows UP" before every rep
> - Full depth, vertical torso, drive up through mid-foot
> - 3 reps per set — quick and clean. No grinding. If rep 2 feels like RPE 7+, drop to 90 kg for set 3
> - Rest 90 sec between sets
>
> **After this morning's deadlifts:**
> Your posterior chain (hamstrings, glutes, lower back) carried the load this morning with tempo pulls and paused deadlifts at 150 kg. Front squats shift the demand to quads and thoracic extension — different muscle groups. Your legs should still be fresh enough for 3x3. If your lower back feels fatigued, use a belt for these sets.
>
> **Left knee consideration:**
> Front squat is a knee-forward position. At 100 kg this should be well within tolerance (you've front squatted 160 kg). If the left knee talks, check foot position — slight toe-out (15-20 degrees) and a touch wider stance can reduce patellar stress.

## Bulgarian Split Squat — MANDATORY

3 x 10 each leg (DB — 12-15 kg each hand)
*Missed in Week 1 Wednesday and Week 2 Wednesday. This is the third scheduled opportunity and the left knee asymmetry correction depends on it. This cannot be pushed again.*

| Set | Leg   | Weight   | Reps  | RPE | Notes |
| --- | ----- | -------- | ----- | --- | ----- |
| 1   | Left  | 12 kg KB | 10/10 | 6   | Slow tempo, kneecap aligned with toe direction |
| 1   | Right | 12 kg KB | 10/10 | 6   | Same cues |
| 2   | Left  | 12 kg KB | 10/10 | 6   | Smooth, sore but no pain |
| 2   | Right | 12 kg KB | 10/10 | 6   |       |
| 3   | Left  | 12 kg KB | 10/10 | 6   | No grinding, no patellar pain |
| 3   | Right | 12 kg KB | 10/10 | 6   |       |

> [!info]- Bulgarian Split Squat — Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Why this is mandatory:**
> The left knee patellar tracking issue is likely driven by VMO/quad imbalance between left and right. Bulgarian split squats load each leg independently — you'll immediately feel which side is weaker. Two weeks without this means the asymmetry correction hasn't started. The Peterson step-downs (done Tue/Fri) target VMO at low load; BSS targets the quad at working load under a full range of motion. Both are needed.
>
> **Execution:**
> - Stand about 2 feet in front of a bench, facing away. Place the top of your rear foot on the bench
> - Hold a dumbbell in each hand at your sides (12-15 kg each)
> - **Left leg first** — always train the weaker side first when fresh
> - Descend by bending your front knee until your back knee nearly touches the floor
> - Front knee tracks over toes — don't let it cave inward
> - Drive back up through your front heel
> - Match reps on right leg
>
> **Left knee management:**
> - Slight toe-out on the front foot (15-20 degrees) reduces patellar stress
> - If you feel patellar pain, check foot position first — often 5 degrees more toe-out resolves it
> - Depth should be challenging but pain-free. If pain appears at full depth, stop 2 inches higher
> - RPE target: 6-7 per leg. If the left leg is noticeably harder (RPE 8+ while right is 6), that's the asymmetry confirming itself — note the gap but don't increase weight to compensate
>
> **After front squats:**
> Your quads are pre-fatigued from the 3x3 front squats. The BSS may feel harder than expected — that's fine. If 15 kg DBs push RPE above 8 on the left leg, drop to 10 kg. Volume (3x10) matters more than load here.

| 3   | Right | 12 kg KB | 10/10 | 6   |       |

> [!info]- Single-Leg RDL — Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Why this matters:**
> Exposes hip stability differences between left and right while strengthening the posterior chain unilaterally. The left side tends to be weaker — this builds balanced hamstring and glute strength that supports the clean pull and deadlift.
>
> **Execution:**
> - Stand on one leg, hold a dumbbell in the opposite hand (contralateral loading)
> - Hinge forward at the hip, back flat, reaching the dumbbell toward the floor
> - Free leg extends behind you, forming a straight line from head to heel
> - Deep hamstring stretch on the standing leg, then drive hips forward to stand
> - Left leg first, match reps on right
>
> **After the deadlifts this morning:**
> Your hamstrings and lower back already worked hard with tempo and paused pulls at 150 kg. Single-leg RDLs at 10-15 kg are a different stimulus — balance-dominant, light load, high rep. If your lower back feels tight, go lighter (10 kg or bodyweight) and focus on the hip hinge pattern.
>
> **Balance tip:** If balance is a major limiter, lightly touch a wall or rack with your free hand — balance, not support.

- [x] Right side: 2 x 45 sec ✅ 2026-04-09

> [!info]- Hip Flexor Stretch — Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Why:**
> Tight hip flexors pull your pelvis into anterior tilt, limiting your ability to stay upright in the squat catch position (both clean and snatch). This directly supports the receiving position quality you're building in Phase 1.
>
> **Execution:**
> - Attach a heavy band to a low rig point behind you
> - Step into the band so it sits in your hip crease
> - Step forward into a half-kneeling position
> - The band pulls your hip forward and down, creating traction
> - Squeeze the glute on the kneeling side, lean slightly forward
> - Hold and breathe. 45 seconds per side, 2 rounds

> This is a milestone. Two weeks of missed BSS meant the left knee asymmetry correction hadn't started. Today's session is the actual Day 1 of that process. Key observations:
> - Both legs at RPE 6 with 12 kg KBs — no obvious left-right gap at this load. This doesn't mean the asymmetry isn't there; 12 kg is likely too light to expose it. As load increases over the coming weeks (15 kg, then 20 kg), the gap will show up as an RPE differential.

> - Sore but no pain — perfect. The soreness is from unaccustomed stimulus (DOMS incoming tomorrow). This will diminish with consistency.
>
> **Single-Leg RDL — Balance and Hamstring Findings**
> Two signals here:
> 1. **Balance shakiness** — expected after a long gap in unilateral standing work. This improves rapidly with exposure (2-3 weeks of consistent practice). The fact that you stayed freestanding without support is good — proprioceptive challenge is the training stimulus.
> 2. **Hamstring tightness limiting rear leg extension** — this connects to the front squat bottom position finding. Tight hamstrings pull the pelvis into posterior tilt at squat depth AND limit the hip extension range on the standing leg during SL-RDL. Both findings point to the same mobility chain. The banded hip flexor stretch you did at the end directly addresses this.
>
> **Cross-Reference: Lumbar-Pelvic Constraint + Hamstring Tightness**
> These two findings from the same session are almost certainly related:
> - Front squat bottom: pelvis tucks under (posterior tilt) because hamstrings are pulling the sit bones down
> - SL-RDL: can't extend the rear leg straight because the hamstrings on the standing leg are limiting hip flexion range
>
> This is a mobility finding, not a strength or pain finding. It doesn't change the program — the hip flexor stretches and 90/90 hip rotations already in the accessory work target this. But it's worth a dedicated conversation to understand what you're feeling and whether any targeted mobility should be added.
