> [!info]- Pause Snatch Complex — Mechanism & Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Why the pause snatch replaces the hang snatch?**
> Week 1's complex started from the hang (above knee) to teach you where the bar should be when you initiate the second pull. You learned that — your drive position migrated higher toward the hip crease by Friday. Now we add the next layer: a pause at the knee, which teaches you *how to transition* from the first pull (floor to knee) to the second pull (knee to hip). The pause removes all momentum — you have to restart the pull from a dead stop at the most mechanically awkward point in the lift. This builds:
> 1. **Positional discipline:** Can you hold a good position at the knee — shins vertical, shoulders over the bar, weight in mid-foot — for 2 seconds without fidgeting or shifting?
> 2. **Patience off the floor:** If you rush the first pull, you'll arrive at the knee in a bad position. The pause punishes that immediately.
> 3. **Acceleration from the transition:** From a dead stop at the knee, you have to accelerate the bar to the hip crease. This forces you to use your legs and hips properly — you can't fake it with arm pull from a dead stop.
>
> **How to execute the pause snatch:**
> - Pull from the floor slowly (same first pull as always). At the knee, STOP. The bar touches or is just above your kneecaps, shins vertical, shoulders over the bar.
> - Hold for a full 2-count. Don't let your hips rise or your shoulders drift forward. Stay tight.
> - After 2 seconds, continue the pull — sweep the bar into the hip crease, extend hard, pull under.
> - **New this week:** Ride the bar down into a full overhead squat even if you could catch it in a power position. This is intentional practice for the receiving position.
>
> **The snatch from floor (rep 2):**
> Same as Week 1 — full lift, no pause. After doing the pause snatch, the full lift should feel fast and smooth because your nervous system just practiced the position. Apply the same squat-receive cue.
>
> **OHS (rep 3):**
> Catch the snatch, then squat again. Same purpose as Week 1 — build overhead stability under fatigue. If you already caught in a full squat, you're essentially doing a partial squat recovery and then a full OHS.
>
> **At 76 kg (72%):**
> This is 3 kg more than Week 1. The pause makes it feel heavier than the number suggests — RPE should be 6-7. If it's 8+, the pause position is wrong (address position before dropping weight). The 6th set is new this week — it should still feel manageable. If set 6 is a grind, that's Week 2 fatigue showing up and is expected.
>
> **Athlete-specific notes:**
> - Watch your drive position — did the higher contact point from Week 1 carry over, or did it reset? If it reset, the pause snatch should help rebuild it because the dead stop at the knee forces a proper transition.
> - Left side: check if the left arm is stable in the overhead squat catch. Week 1 didn't flag issues here, but with 3 more kg and an extra set, thoracic fatigue may appear.

> [!info]- Tempo Back Squat — Mechanism & Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Week 2 context:**
> Week 1 exposed that tempo adds ~10-15% effective load. 70% (133 kg) with a 3-1-0 tempo was RPE 8-9 — way above the RPE 6-7 target. You dropped to 125 kg mid-session and still couldn't complete all reps. This week we start at 60% (115 kg) and hold it there. The goals remain the same as Week 1:
>
> **1. Tendon adaptation for the left knee**
> The 3-second eccentric loads the patellar tendon under controlled, sustained tension. Your knee tracked 4-5/5 across Week 1, which is encouraging — the rehab-level loading is in the right zone. Don't increase squat weight just because it feels "easy" — the tendon adaptation happens at moderate loads over time, not from pushing to RPE 9.
>
> **2. Bar position exploration**
> You noted on Day 1 that you're still finding your low bar position — higher (above scapular spine) feels more powerful but hurts the back; lower (below scapular spine) is less stressful but feels weaker. This is a common transition issue. This week, try this: **set the bar just below the top of the scapular spine** — not as low as a powerlifting low bar, but not a high bar either. Your body type (181 cm, 92 kg) usually does well with a "mid-low" position. Lock it in this week and stop adjusting between sets.
>
> **3. Tempo execution**
> Same as Week 1: 3 seconds down (count it), 1 second dead stop at the bottom, fast up. The dead stop eliminates the stretch reflex — you have to generate force from nothing. This directly trains the bottom of the clean catch. If the pause makes it too hard at 115 kg, you can switch to 3-0-0 (no pause) at the same weight before dropping weight — the pause accounts for ~5% of the effective difficulty.
>
> **Expected RPE at 115 kg:**

> [!note] Coach's Review — Day 6 (Week 2 Tuesday)
>
> **Snatch Complex — Good Session, OHS Stability Is the Development Area**
> 76 kg at RPE 6.5 is exactly where it should be. You followed the squat-receive cue — that's a habit being built. The pause at the knee felt controlled, the full snatch felt smooth, and the explosive position carried over from Week 1. All strong signals.
>
> The OHS instability is worth dissecting. You're using a ~3-second descent on the OHS and feeling shaky, stepping forward in 2 of your sets. Here's what's happening: the pause snatch and full snatch fatigue your upper back and shoulders. By the time you reach the OHS (rep 3 of the complex), thoracic extension is harder to maintain, and the bar drifts forward. The forward step is a compensation — your center of mass moves forward and you chase it. This is a thoracic endurance issue, not a strength issue. **Fix: on the OHS portion, don't use a slow tempo. Just squat at normal speed — controlled but not deliberately slow. The OHS in the complex is a stability check, not a tempo exercise. Save thoracic energy for the catch position.**
>
> The elbow cue (pushing through the bar instead of gripping hard) reducing medial elbow pain is an important finding. A death grip creates excessive wrist flexor/forearm tension that refers to the medial epicondyle. "Push up into the bar" is the correct overhead cue — it activates the triceps and shoulder stabilizers rather than the forearm flexors. Keep using this cue on all overhead work. This should also help with OHS stability because the bar sits more actively on the palm/heel of the hand rather than being crushed in a fist.
>
> Adding sets 7-8 to compensate for the set 2 OHS miss shows good self-awareness. One note: the compensation was generous — set 2 was only a partial miss (snatch portions were fine, only the OHS dropped). Two extra sets means 2 additional full complexes, which is 8 total vs the prescribed 6. That's fine this week at 76 kg / RPE 6.5 — it's not excessive volume. But at heavier loads, be cautious about adding compensatory sets. A partial miss on one component of a complex doesn't always need full-complex compensation.
>
> **Back Squat — Answering Your Two Questions**
>
> *1. Breathing strategy for tempo squats:*
> Your current approach — one breath, 3 seconds down, hold, up — means you're holding your breath for 5-6 seconds per rep. That's too long. You're oxygen-depleted by mid-set and your RPE is inflated by the breathing deficit, not just the load. Here's the fix:
>
> **Breathe at the top of every rep, not at the bottom.** The sequence:
> 1. Stand at the top. Take a big breath, brace hard (360-degree pressure).
> 2. Descend for 3 seconds — hold breath, maintain brace.
> 3. Pause 1 second at the bottom — still holding.
> 4. Drive up explosively — exhale through pursed lips as you pass the sticking point (about halfway up). Don't release all the air at once — a controlled exhale keeps some intra-abdominal pressure.
> 5. At the top, **fully stand up, take 1-2 breaths to recover, then re-brace for the next rep.**
>
> The key change: **you're allowed to breathe between reps at the top.** Tempo squats are not touch-and-go. Stand all the way up, breathe, re-brace, then start the next rep. Each rep is its own event. This might add 3-5 seconds per set — that's fine. The extra rest at the top means you arrive at each rep with adequate oxygen and a fresh brace. RPE should drop 1-1.5 points from this change alone.
>
> Do not breathe at the bottom. Releasing the brace in the hole under load is dangerous — your lumbar spine loses its stiffness and the bar can crush you forward. Only breathe at the top, standing fully upright.
>
> *2. Should you switch to high bar? Why does 115 kg feel so hard?*
> Three factors are stacking:
>
> - **Tempo tax** (~10-15% effective load): 115 kg with 3-1-0 feels like 130-132 kg of normal squatting. Your normal low bar squat max is 190 kg, so this is effectively ~68-70% of max. That's a reasonable RPE 7-8 territory — closer to what you're reporting than you think.
> - **Sunday WOD carryover**: 15 power snatches at 61 kg + 25 OHS + 60 HSPU the day before is real residual fatigue. Your legs and shoulders were not fresh today. This alone could account for 0.5-1 RPE point.
> - **Breathing deficit**: As discussed above, running out of air mid-set is inflating RPE by at least 1 point.
>
> If you fix the breathing and come in without Sunday fatigue, 115 kg with tempo would likely be RPE 6.5-7 — right on target. **Don't switch to high bar.** The issue isn't bar position. Low bar is correct for your goals (back squat strength is priority #2, and low bar lets you move more weight). Switching bar positions mid-program adds a new variable that masks the real data. Instead: fix the breathing, avoid heavy metcons the day before squat day, and reassess RPE next week.
>
> That said, I've adjusted Week 3's back squat from 124 kg to 110 kg as a safety net. If the breathing fix brings 115 kg to RPE 7, we can bump Week 3 back up. Better to have room to increase than to keep grinding at RPE 8+.
>
> **Peterson Step-Down — Left-Right Asymmetry Is Real and Expected**
>
> The left knee can't tolerate a full-depth step-down with vertical torso at 20 cm + 8 kg KB. The right side handles it easily. This is the clearest marker of the patellar tracking issue — the left VMO can't control the knee through end-range under load without compensating through trunk flexion.
>
> **Answers to your questions:**
> - **Vertical torso vs trunk flexion:** Vertical torso is the ideal — it maximizes VMO loading and mimics the knee-forward demands of squatting and jerking. Trunk flexion shifts load to the hip extensors and reduces patellar tendon demand, making it easier but less targeted. For now: **use a vertical torso on the right side (training stimulus), allow slight trunk flexion on the left side (pain management), and work toward matching the right side over weeks.**
> - **Lower the step:** Yes. Drop the left side to 10-15 cm — whatever height lets you do 10 reps with a mostly vertical torso and no pain above a 3/10. Keep the right side at 20 cm. Asymmetric step heights are fine and actually smarter — you're dosing each side to its current capacity. Also, **drop the KB on the left side back to bodyweight at the lower step height.** Progress the left side independently: first master the lower step bodyweight with vertical torso, then add the KB, then increase step height. The right side can continue progressing normally.
