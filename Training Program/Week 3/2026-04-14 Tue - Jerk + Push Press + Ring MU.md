> [!info]- Push Jerk — Mechanism & Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Week 3 progression:**
> From 86 kg to 90 kg (+4.7%), and from 5 sets to 6. This is the biggest load and volume jump for the jerk so far. At 86 kg, RPE was 6-6.5 — well within the target. 90 kg should land at RPE 6.5-7.5. The extra set at 90 kg is the peak volume stimulus before deload.
>
> **Dip mechanics — the primary development area:**
> Week 2 findings:
> - **Dip depth inconsistency:** varies rep-to-rep. The calibration drill in warm-up addresses this.
> - **COM forward drift:** pressure shifts to toes during the dip, causing bar to travel forward and loading the front of the knee. The "heels heavy" cue directly counters this.
> - **Jerk from rack vs from clean:** Friday's C&J (from squat clean) had zero knee pain at 95 kg; Thursday's rack jerk at 86 kg had 2-3/10 pain. Hypothesis: the squat clean sets up better thoracic engagement for the jerk. From the rack, consciously set the thoracic position before each set: elbows UP, chest UP, take a breath that lifts the rib cage, then dip.
>
> **"Heels heavy through the dip" — what this means mechanically:**
> When you initiate the dip, imagine pushing the floor away through your whole foot — heels, balls of feet, and toes all pressing equally. If at any point during the dip you feel the pressure shift forward to the toes, STOP — the dip has gone forward. The correction: think of sitting your hips straight down, as if between your heels, rather than bending the knees forward.
>
> **Wider grip on sets 5-6:**

Today tests the wider grip at the new working weight (90 kg). Use sets 1-4 as baseline, then compare. If wider grip changes the dip feel or lockout quality, note it.
>
> **Expected RPE at 90 kg:** RPE 6.5-7.5. If sets 1-2 are 7.5+, check: is the bar traveling straight up? Is the dip going forward? If mechanics are good and it's just heavy, drop to 86 kg. No shame — this is a 4 kg jump.

> [!info]- Push Press — Mechanism & Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Why push press instead of strict press this week?**
> Strict press at 52 kg (Week 2) trains raw pressing strength. Push press at 65-70 kg trains the dip-drive-lockout sequence at heavier loads — the same motor pattern as the jerk but slower and more controllable. This is an overload bridge between strict press (no legs) and jerk (full speed). It also gives the shoulders heavier lockout practice.
>
> **Starting weight:**
> Your strict press working weight is 52 kg for 4x6. Push press should be ~25-35% heavier due to leg assistance. Starting at 65 kg (sets 1-2), then 70 kg (sets 3-4) if it feels manageable. If 65 kg is RPE 7+, stay there for all 4 sets.
>
> **Execution:**
> - Bar at front rack, same setup as jerk
> - Dip 3-4 inches (shorter than jerk dip), drive hard through the legs
> - As bar passes the forehead, push head through and press to full lockout
> - **Dead stop to clavicle between reps** — same standard as strict press. Lower the bar, let it settle, re-brace, then dip-drive again. No bouncing.
> - Apply "heels heavy" cue on the dip — same as the jerk
>
> **Grip width:**
> Use clean grip for all sets. The push press is a training tool, not a grip experiment. Keep the variable count low — save grip exploration for the jerk.
>
> **Expected RPE:** 6-7 at 65 kg, 7-8 at 70 kg. If 70 kg pushes above RPE 8, stay at 65 kg.

**Athlete Notes:**
Felt strong on push press — not hard, even at 70 kg. One issue: bar rolling in the palm during the press. Possible cause: elbows are set high in the front rack (needed for the dip), which positions the bar further back on the hand. Same right-side compensation as the jerk — relying more on right side during the drive.

> [!info]- Ring Muscle-Up Progressions — Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Week 3 progression — restructured:**
> - Ring dips: 6 → 8 reps per set. Same slow negative (3 sec down). Building endurance in the bottom position, which mimics the MU transition depth.
> - **False grip pull-up 3x3 (NEW):** Hang from rings in false grip, pull chin above the rings. If you can't pull to the rings, use a band. This tests whether the false grip is now strong enough to hold under pulling load. Week 2's false grip hang felt "more adapted" — this is the next test.
> - **False grip ring rows to sternum 3x5 (NEW — replaces banded transitions):** Feet on floor, lean back, pull your sternum to the rings while maintaining false grip. This teaches the exact pulling path of the muscle-up transition (pull high to sternum, not just chin) without the coordination demand of the kip. Progression: once 3x8 is comfortable, re-introduce banded transitions.
>
> **Why this restructure?**
> The banded ring MU transitions weren't clicking after 2 weeks. Rather than forcing a complex coordination pattern, we break it into components: (1) false grip endurance (hang → pull-ups), (2) high pull path (ring rows to sternum), (3) dip strength (ring dips). When each component is strong, the full transition becomes learnable.
>
> **False grip hang removed:**
> You've been doing false grip hangs for 2 weeks and it's progressed well. The false grip pull-up subsumes this — if you can pull in false grip, you can hang in false grip. No need for both.
