> [!info]- Snatch Complex — Mechanism & Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Why this 3-position complex?**
> Week 1 used hang snatch (above knee). Week 2 added a pause at the knee. Week 3 combines both AND adds a hang from below the knee — the hardest starting position. Each variant trains a different aspect:
>
> 1. **Hang snatch from below the knee:** You start with the bar just below the kneecap, shins slightly angled, shoulders over the bar. From here, you must sweep the bar back into the thigh and accelerate through the hip crease. This trains the exact transition where your bar was drifting away from the thigh on Friday. There's zero momentum from the floor — all initiation comes from the legs and back. If you can keep the bar close from below the knee, it'll stay close from the floor.
>
> 2. **Pause snatch at the knee:** Same as Week 2. Pull from the floor, pause 2 seconds at the knee, then continue. The pause reinforces patience and positional discipline. Coming after the hang snatch, your body has already rehearsed the below-knee position — the pause should feel more dialed in.
>
> 3. **Full snatch from the floor:** No pauses, no hangs. Put it all together. After the two positional variants, the full lift should feel fast and automatic. Apply the squat-receive cue.
>
> **At 79 kg (75%):**
> Same weight as Week 2 Friday's working sets, but the complex is harder (3 snatches per set vs 2 singles). RPE should be 6.5-7.5. If it's 8+, the accumulated fatigue from three variants is too high — drop to 76 kg.
>
> **New cues for Week 3:**
> - **"Sweep the bar into the hip"** — after the bar passes the knee, actively pull it back toward your body. Think of your lats engaging to keep the bar on the thighs. This prevents the forward drift you noticed on Friday sets 4-5.
> - **"Knuckles to ceiling"** — at the catch, actively press up with the heel of the hand. This prevents the bar from rolling toward the palm (the issue from Friday). Your wrist stays slightly extended, the bar sits in the fingers/heel of hand, and the overhead position is more stable.
> - **Maintain hook grip through the catch** on rep 1 (the hang snatch) of at least 3 sets. This forces the bar to sit in the fingers. Release the hook on the pause snatch and full lift if needed.
>
> **Peak volume note:**
> 6 sets x 3 snatches per complex = 18 total snatch reps. This is the most snatch volume in a single session so far. Quality will degrade on later sets — that's expected. If sets 5-6 feel significantly sloppier than sets 1-3, that's fatigue, not regression.
>
> **Athlete-specific notes:**
> - Squat-receive every rep. This is now the default — don't revert to power catching.
> - Watch for the bar rolling to the palm on later sets when you're fatigued — that's when the hook grip cue matters most.
> - After the weekend rest, your body should feel fresher than Friday's session. Use that freshness to really nail the below-knee position.

> [!info]- Tempo Back Squat — Mechanism & Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Week 3 context:**
> Week 2 at 115 kg was RPE 8-8.5 — still above the 6-7 target. We've dropped 5 kg to 110 kg but added a 5th set (peak volume). The net work is slightly higher (5x5 = 25 reps vs 4x6 = 24 reps) but each individual set is shorter (5 reps vs 6). The load reduction should bring RPE down.
>
> **THE BREATHING FIX — this is the primary intervention:**
> Week 2's elevated RPE was partly caused by holding your breath too long. Here's the corrected sequence:
>
> 1. **Stand at the top. Take a big breath, brace hard (360-degree pressure around your midsection).**
> 2. Descend for 3 seconds — hold breath, maintain brace.
> 3. Pause 1 second at the bottom — still holding.
> 4. Drive up explosively — exhale through pursed lips as you pass the sticking point (about halfway up). Don't release all air at once — controlled exhale keeps some intra-abdominal pressure.
> 5. **At the top, FULLY STAND UP. Take 1-2 breaths to recover. Re-brace. Then start the next rep.**
>
> The key change: **you are allowed to breathe between reps at the top.** Each rep is its own event. This might add 3-5 seconds per set — that's fine. RPE should drop 1-1.5 points from this change alone.
>
> **Do NOT breathe at the bottom.** Releasing the brace in the hole under load is dangerous.
>
> **Bar position:**
> Settled on "mid-low" position from Week 2 — just below the top of the scapular spine. Don't adjust between sets. Lock it in.
>
> **The test:**
> If 110 kg with the breathing fix is RPE 6-7, the breathing was the issue all along. If it's still RPE 8+, we investigate further (bar position experiment or 1RM recalibration in Week 5 test week).
>
> **Expected RPE at 110 kg with proper breathing:** RPE 6-7. Each set of 5 with the 3-1-0 tempo should take ~25-30 seconds of work, plus 5-10 seconds of breathing at the top between reps. Total time under tension per set: ~35-40 seconds.

The goal is to build the habit gradually — full hook-through-catch will come over weeks, not days.

> This is the headline result today. Week 2: 115 kg, 4x6, RPE 8-8.5. Week 3: 110 kg, 5x5, RPE 7-7.5. The breathing fix accounts for the majority of that RPE drop. >
> **Eye gaze during squats:** Good instinct to question this. Looking up during the drive is a common compensation — it hyperextends the cervical spine, which reflexively engages the upper back extensors and creates a momentary feeling of "easier." The problem: it pulls the spine out of neutral alignment under load. At 110 kg this is low-risk, but as loads climb toward 150+ kg, cervical hyperextension under heavy load creates unnecessary shear force and can mask thoracic weakness.
>
> **The correct gaze protocol for low-bar tempo back squats:**
> - **Descent:** pick a spot on the floor about 2-3 meters ahead of you. Keep your eyes on that spot as you descend. This maintains a neutral neck position that follows the natural torso angle.
> - **Bottom/pause:** same spot. Don't look down further — just hold the gaze.
> - **Drive up:** same spot. As your torso rises, your gaze will naturally rise with it. **Do not deliberately look up** — let the gaze follow the torso angle. If you feel the urge to look up, it usually means your chest is dropping and your body is trying to compensate. The fix isn't the eyes — it's driving the chest up through the sticking point.
> - The cue: **"chest up, eyes follow"** — lead with the chest, and the gaze follows naturally.
>
> Try this on Tuesday's session and compare. If it feels harder, that's the compensation being removed — which means you're now doing the work with the correct muscles.
