> [!info]- Snatch (Full Lifts) — Mechanism & Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Week 3 progression — heaviest snatch session of Phase 1:**
> - Heavy single: 79 kg (Wk1) → 81 kg (Wk2) → 84 kg (Wk3). That's 80% of your 105 kg max and only 1 kg below the snatch-to-bodyweight ratio threshold.
> - Working sets: consistent at 79 kg across all 3 weeks, but the context changes — after a heavier single, 79 kg should feel relatively lighter on the working sets.
>
> **Building to 84 kg:**
> Build up gradually with more intermediate steps than Week 2. Suggested: empty bar → 40 → 50 → 60 → 70 → 76 → 80 → 84 kg. The 80 kg single before 84 kg gives you a confidence check. If 80 kg doesn't feel right, stop there — 80 kg is still a Week 2+ heavy single.
>
> **84 kg considerations:**
> This is the heaviest snatch in the program so far. At 80%, there's less margin for positional error:
> - The bar MUST stay close (sweep into hip cue). Any forward drift at this load = missed lift.
> - The turnover must be fast. At 84 kg, you can't muscle it overhead — you must pull yourself under.
> - Squat-receive is now a necessity, not just a practice cue. 84 kg is likely too heavy to power snatch with good positions.
>
> **Working sets at 79 kg after 84 kg:**
> After a heavier single, working sets feel relatively lighter — this is a potentiation effect. RPE on the 79 kg doubles should be 6-7 (down from the 6.5-7 at 79 kg in Week 2). Use this to focus on execution quality: bar path, catch position, hook grip maintenance.
>
> **End-of-peak-volume fatigue:**
> This is day 5 of peak volume week. Monday's snatch complex (18 total reps), Tuesday's jerk + push press, Wednesday's clean + deadlift, Thursday's front squat + OHS + metcon — it's all accumulated. If you feel slower or less explosive than usual, that's normal. The Week 4 deload is designed to let this fatigue dissipate. Don't let a "heavy day" feeling deter you from attempting 84 kg — try it. If it doesn't work, take 80 kg as your single and move on.
>
> **Cue checklist per rep:**
> 1. Before the pull: "sweep into hip" — set the intent to keep the bar close
> 2. At the catch: "knuckles to ceiling" — press up with heel of hand
> 3. At the bottom: don't rush to stand. Stabilize, feel the position, then stand.

> [!info]- Handstand Walk Progressions — Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Week 3 progression:**
> - Wall holds: 35 → 40 sec. Shoulder endurance in the inverted position continues building. If 40 sec feels easy, move hands closer to the wall for a more vertical body.
> - Shoulder taps: 6 → 8 each hand. Two more reps per side. Go slow — control over speed.

> [!note] Coach's Review — Day 15 (Week 3 Friday)
>
> **Snatch — 84 kg single @ RPE 7. This is the headline result of the week.**
> 84 kg is a Phase 1 milestone: the heaviest snatch attempted in the program so far, 80% of your 105 kg max, and you caught it smoothly with hook grip at RPE 7. Fatigue, hunger, and a flat energy state going in — and the top single still felt like it did. That's a strong signal about your actual snatch ceiling: when the positional cues click (bar high, hook grip, squat-receive), the weight isn't the limiter. The snatch-to-squat ratio gap is confirmed as positional/technical, not strength.
>
> **But the working sets tell a different story — and it's not the +1 kg.**
> You used 80 kg instead of 79 kg. That's 76% vs 75% — negligible, same story as Monday's snatch complex. The real story is **3 missed reps, all via the same failure mode: "bar fell forward" out of the catch.** Set 3 rep 1, set 6 reps 2 and 3. Three data points, one pattern.
>
> This is a classic fatigue + bar-path signature. You described feeling the bar "driven high" on every working set — but when you're fatigued, drive-high often means drive-forward. The second pull at the hip starts to lose its vertical component; your legs don't extend as hard, and your back extensors take over — which yanks the bar forward instead of straight up. The bar ends up in front of your center of mass at the catch, and no amount of "knuckles to ceiling" can fix a bar that's already forward. The fix isn't more aggression; it's the opposite — **stay patient in the pull, finish the hip drive, let the bar find your body.**
>
> **The compensation set (set 6) was a mistake — I want to flag this honestly.**
> Missing 1 rep out of 10 across a working set is a successful session, not a failure. Adding a 6th set after RPE 8.5 on set 5, during day 5 of peak volume week, on a fatigued nervous system — that's trying to "earn back" the rep, and the result was 2 more missed reps reinforcing the bar-forward pattern. Going forward: **if you miss a rep, note it, stay on schedule, move on.** The working set target was 10/10; 9/10 is a pass. Chasing the miss at 80% is how pattern errors get grooved in. In Phase 2 when loads climb, this matters more — build the habit now.

> **C&J — 3/5 made at 98 kg, and a new shoulder finding that needs attention.**
>
> Let me separate the two issues, because they're unrelated and the shoulder one is the bigger deal.
>
> **Issue 1 — misses from pattern + fatigue.**

> - The two made-at-Wide-grip sets (3 yes, 4 no) are too small a sample to draw conclusions about the grip experiment. Inconclusive.
>
> **Issue 2 — anterior shoulder pull on the squat clean pull, absent on power clean. This is new.**
> You noticed a stretch/pull on the anterior shoulder joint during the squat clean pull, soreness-level. NOT present at 120 kg power cleans on Wednesday. That's a meaningful asymmetry because the first and second pulls of a squat clean and power clean are mechanically identical up to extension. So what changes?
>
> **The difference is the third pull — the pull under the bar.** In a power clean, you finish extending and let the bar settle onto the shelf; your arms barely work. In a squat clean, you actively pull yourself under the bar as it rises, which loads the anterior shoulder capsule as the arms internally rotate and the bar clears your face. If your bar path is slightly forward (which your misses suggest it is today), the bar passes further from your body on the way up, and the shoulder has to reach forward/up to catch — stressing the anterior capsule. Combined with your known thoracic mobility restriction (limits front rack, which cascades into how the shoulder has to compensate to catch), this is a plausible mechanical explanation.
>

If yes, this is a real pattern issue and we'll address it with pre-clean mobility and catch-position drills in Week 4 deload. If no, it may have been a fatigue/heavy-load artifact from a decayed pattern. Either answer is useful. I've noted this in the Saturday day-focus.
