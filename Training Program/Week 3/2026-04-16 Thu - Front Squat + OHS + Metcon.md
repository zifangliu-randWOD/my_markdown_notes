> [!info]- Tempo Front Squat — Mechanism & Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Week 3 context:**
> Week 1: 112 kg with 3-0-0 tempo was too heavy (RPE 8-9 after dropping from 112 to 105-100). Week 2 PM: 100 kg no tempo was RPE 6. Today: 107 kg with 3-0-0 tempo — a 7 kg increase from the PM session, but the tempo adds effective load. The 3-0-0 tempo (no pause at bottom) is lighter than Monday's 3-1-0 back squat tempo, so this should be more manageable.
>
> **The effective load math:**
> 107 kg with 3-0-0 tempo feels approximately like 120-123 kg at normal speed (tempo adds ~12-15% effective load). Your front squat max is 160 kg, so this is effectively ~75-77% — which should be RPE 7-7.5. Manageable but not easy, especially on 5x5.
>
> **Front rack position — the real training stimulus:**
> At 107 kg for sets of 5 with a 3-second eccentric, your thoracic extensors are under sustained tension. By sets 4-5, the elbows will want to drop. The cue: **"elbows UP"** before every rep. If the elbows drop, the bar shifts forward, your torso rounds, and the squat turns into a good morning. Re-set the elbows at the top of each rep during your breathing pause.
>
> **Lumbar-pelvic constraint (butt wink):**
> Week 2 PM at 100 kg noted a lumbar-pelvic constraint at the bottom. At 107 kg this may be more noticeable. Management:
> - The 90/90 breathing in warm-up addresses the pelvic position
> - Don't force depth beyond where the pelvis starts to tuck. Go to the depth you can maintain a neutral spine. If that's 1-2 inches above full depth, that's fine for now.
> - The 3-second descent should actually help — it gives you time to feel the tuck point and stop there
>
> **After yesterday's deadlifts:**
> Your posterior chain (hamstrings, glutes, lower back) worked hard yesterday with 5x5 tempo deadlifts at 150 kg. Front squats shift demand to quads and thoracic extension. Your quads should be relatively fresh, but your lower back may carry fatigue. If the lower back feels tight, use a belt for these sets.
>
> **Expected RPE at 107 kg (3-0-0):** RPE 7-7.5. If it's 8+ on set 1, drop to 100 kg immediately — don't push through 5 sets at that RPE.

> [!info]- Overhead Squat — Mechanism & Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Why OHS in the program?**
> The overhead squat is the receiving position for the snatch. By training it as a standalone movement at moderate load, you build the specific overhead stability and mobility needed for heavier snatch catches. This directly supports the snatch efficiency gap (55% snatch-to-squat ratio) — the deeper and more stable your OHS position, the higher you can catch the snatch.
>
> **Week 3 at 68 kg (65% of snatch):**
> Up from Week 1's 63 kg. This is still light enough that it shouldn't be a strength challenge. The work is positional: can you maintain an active overhead position through 20 total reps (4x5)?
>
> **Connection to Week 2 snatch findings:**
> On Friday you noticed bar rolling toward the palm and forward lean at the catch. The OHS is where you can practice the fix: "knuckles to ceiling," active press up through the heel of the hand, maintain hook grip on at least one set. If you can hold a solid overhead position here at 68 kg, the snatch catch at 79-84 kg will be better.
>
> **Execution:**
> - Take the bar from the rack or snatch it to overhead
> - Snatch-width grip, active press up, slight shrug
> - Descend at a controlled pace (not slow tempo, just no rushing)
> - Full depth — or to the depth where the lumbar-pelvic constraint kicks in, whichever comes first
> - Stand fully between reps, maintaining the overhead position
>
> **After the front squats:**
> Your quads are pre-fatigued. The OHS loads the quads differently (more evenly distributed between quads, posterior chain, and overhead stabilizers), so this should feel lighter than the front squats. If overhead stability degrades by set 3-4, the shoulders are fatigued from Tuesday's push press and jerk work — that's expected at peak volume.

> [!info]- Metcon — Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Why this is here:**
> Conditioning is priority #5 (lowest) but it still needs periodic maintenance. This is the first real metcon since Week 1. The movement selection is intentional:
>
> - **Wall balls:** Combines a front squat with an overhead press. At 9 kg this is very light but taxes the legs and shoulders under fatigue — directly relevant to your jerk dip + drive pattern. Knee-friendly at this load.
> - **Toes-to-bar:** Core and hip flexor work under fatigue. Also tests grip after the clean and deadlift work this week. This is a gymnastics movement that requires kipping efficiency — a skill worth maintaining.
>
> **Pacing:**
> This is NOT a competition piece. Target: 6-8 minutes. Break the round of 21 if needed (11/10 or 7/7/7). The goal is to finish with a moderate heart rate, not gasping on the floor. If anything feels off in the knees during wall balls (unlikely at 9 kg), switch to goblet squats.
>
> **Expected RPE:** 7-8 on the cardiovascular side. Muscular RPE should be low — these are light loads. If you're still breathing hard after 5 minutes post-metcon, the conditioning has decayed more than expected — note it but don't worry about it.

> [!info]- Accessory — Mechanism & Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Thoracic Extension Over Bench 3x10 breaths**
> Kneel in front of a bench, place elbows on the bench with hands together (prayer position), sit your hips back and let your chest sink toward the floor. Breathe deeply — each exhale lets you sink a little deeper. This is more aggressive than the foam roller version and targets thoracic extension under load (gravity + arm position). Directly supports front rack and jerk overhead positions.
>
> **Ankle Dorsiflexion Stretch 2x30 sec each**
> Face a wall, foot ~4 inches from the wall, push your knee over your toes toward the wall. If your knee touches, move the foot further back. This builds ankle dorsiflexion range — limited ankle mobility contributes to forward lean in squats and the jerk dip. Compare left vs right.
>
> **Banded Overhead Distraction 2x30 sec each arm**
> Attach a band overhead, grip with one hand, step away so the band pulls your arm up and slightly back. This opens the shoulder capsule in the overhead position — directly supports OHS and snatch catch stability. Spend extra time on the left arm.
