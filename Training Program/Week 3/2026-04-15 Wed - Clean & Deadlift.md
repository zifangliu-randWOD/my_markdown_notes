> [!info]- Clean Complex — Mechanism & Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Why hang clean from below the knee?**
> Same principle as Monday's snatch: the below-knee hang removes floor momentum and forces you to initiate the pull from the hardest transition point. For the clean, this trains:
> 1. **Sweep into the thigh:** From below the knee, the bar must travel back into the upper thigh before extension. If you rush, the bar stays forward and the clean feels heavy on the catch.
> 2. **Patience through the transition:** You can't muscle a below-knee hang clean at 105 kg — the legs and hips must do the work. This builds the discipline to wait for the bar to reach the hip crease before extending.
>
> **Squat clean (rep 2):**
> From the floor, full lift. After the hang clean, your body has already rehearsed the below-knee position. The floor lift should feel fast and automatic. Pull yourself under into a full front squat catch.
>
> **At 105 kg (75%):**
> This is 5 kg more than the box session flat weight (100 kg) and 5 kg below your box session top-end (110 kg). But the below-knee hang makes it feel heavier than a floor lift. RPE should be 6.5-7.5 per set. If the hang clean feels like RPE 8+, drop to 100 kg.
>
> **Box session note:**
> If you're at the box and they're running a clean cycle, prioritize squat cleans over power cleans. Week 1 and 2 Wednesdays were both power-clean-only — the squat clean practice gap matters as loads climb. If the box WOD uses power cleans, do 3-4 squat clean singles at 100-105 kg after class.
>
> **Connection to Week 2:**
> You squat-cleaned 95 kg smoothly on Friday and it "felt smooth." 105 kg is a 10 kg jump in a complex format, but you handled 110 kg power cleans at RPE 8.5 in the box. The squat clean receiving position actually makes heavy cleans easier (you don't need to pull as high), so 105 kg squat clean should feel less demanding than 110 kg power clean.
>
> **Front rack consideration:**
> At 105 kg, the bar is heavy enough that elbow speed in the turnover becomes critical. Slow elbows → bar crashes → catch feels heavy → thoracic rounds → elbows drop → bar slides. The cue: "fast elbows, high elbows." Think about whipping the elbows through as the bar contacts the shoulders.

> [!info]- Clean-Grip Deadlift (Tempo) — Mechanism & Coaching Notes (click to expand)
>
> **Week 3 context — peak volume:**
> 5x5 is the most tempo deadlift volume so far (25 reps, up from Week 2's ~12-15 total reps). Each set of 5 at 3-0-3 tempo takes ~30 seconds of continuous tension. 5 sets = 2.5 minutes of total time under tension. This is a significant posterior chain stimulus.
>
> **Why 150 kg, not 156 kg?**
> The phase file originally prescribed 156 kg (78%). Week 2 at 150 kg without belt was RPE 8-9. Going higher would push into grinding territory, which defeats the purpose of tempo work (positional quality, not maximal effort). 150 kg with belt should be RPE 7-8 — hard work but maintainable positions.
>
> **Belt usage:**
> Week 2 showed you can handle 150 kg and 110 kg cleans without belt or sleeves — that built intrinsic stability. Week 3 at peak volume is the time to use the belt for performance, not as a crutch. The belt increases intra-abdominal pressure, which reduces spinal loading and lets you focus on pulling positions rather than bracing capacity.
>
> **Breathing on tempo deadlifts:**
> Same principle as tempo squats: breathe at the top of each rep.
> 1. At lockout, exhale and take 1-2 breaths.
> 2. Re-brace (big breath, 360-degree brace).
> 3. Begin the 3-second descent.
> 4. At the floor, maintain brace, immediately begin the 3-second ascent.
> 5. At lockout, breathe again.
>
> **Execution:**
> - Clean-width grip (hook grip or straps), feet hip-width
> - 3-second pull: push floor away, count "one-one-thousand, two-one-thousand, three-one-thousand" to lockout
> - Lockout: hips fully extended, shoulders back
> - 3-second descent: reverse the pull, maintain back angle
> - Brief reset at floor (touch and go OK if no bounce)
>
> **What to watch:**
> If your back rounds during the ascent, the weight is too heavy or you're fatigued. Drop weight. If the bar drifts forward of mid-foot, you're pulling with the back instead of pushing with the legs. Cue: "push the floor away."
