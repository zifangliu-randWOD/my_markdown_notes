### Warm-up: Mobility Block (~15 min)

**Phase 1 — Reset (3 min)**

- [ ] **90/90 breathing — 2 min** (8–10 slow breath cycles)
- [ ] **Right erector foam roll — 60 sec**

> [!info]- Coaching — Reset
>
> **90/90 Breathing**
> Lie on your back, feet on a wall (or the foot of the bed), knees and hips both at 90°. Every exhale, let the lower back soften into the floor — not forced flat by squeezing the abs, but breath-led relaxation. Inhale into the posterior ribcage (feel the sides and back of the ribcage expand, not the chest). 8–10 slow cycles.
>
> This is the same drill that resolved the lumbar-pelvic constraint in Wk 3 and it stays as the daily foundation because it directly supports the right erector situation: the diaphragm and deep core stabilizers are the muscles that should be doing the pelvic stabilization job. When they're active, the right erector can stop doing it — and that's exactly what the trip protocol is trying to accomplish.
>
> **Expected feel:** quiet and expansive. The low back should feel like it melts into the floor rather than being pressed there. If the right erector feels like it's guarding even while you're lying down, stay for 4–5 extra breath cycles before moving to the foam roll.
>
> **Right Erector Foam Roll**
> No full foam roller needed — the travel foam roller works fine. Find the right erector: the rope-like muscle running 2–3 cm to the right of the spine, between the bottom rib and the iliac crest. Apply firm sustained pressure and work the length of that band for 60 seconds.
>
> Pressure target: 3–4/10 discomfort — firm enough to feel it, never sharp. You're asking the muscle to downshift its resting tone, not trying to crush it. If it feels more irritated after rolling than before, the next day's dose should be lighter (less pressure, shorter time) or just do the cross-friction version (fingertip pressure in small circles rather than roller compression).
>
> **What to log:** does the right erector feel calmer, unchanged, or more irritated after the roll? That response trend across 6 days is the outcome data for this limiter.

**Phase 2 — Hip Mobility (5 min)**

- [ ] **Couch stretch — 60 sec each side**
- [ ] **Pigeon stretch — 60 sec each side, LEFT FIRST**
- [ ] **90/90 hip rotation — 10 reps each side**

> [!info]- Coaching — Hip Mobility
>
> **Couch stretch**
> Standing version: back foot elevated on a chair or the edge of the bed, front foot stepped forward into a long lunge. Squeeze the back-leg glute hard, posteriorly tilt the pelvis (tuck the belt-line slightly upward), and push the hips forward until you feel a deep stretch in the front of the back-leg hip. Hold for 60 seconds each side.
>
> Setup check: ribs down, glute squeezed, lumbar spine neutral — not arched. If you feel the stretch mostly in the low back rather than the front of the hip, you're arching to compensate. Reset: less hip forward angle, more glute squeeze, more pelvic tuck.
>
> Why this matters for the right erector specifically: travel sitting loads the hip flexors into shortened positions for hours. Short hip flexors pull the pelvis into anterior tilt → the lower back extensors (including the right erector) have to work overtime to keep you upright. This is the single highest-leverage movement in the trip protocol for the erector management. It addresses the mechanical cause, not just the symptom.
>
> **Left knee note:** if the back-leg knee feels compressed during the couch stretch, put a folded towel or your yoga block under it. The kneeling position can aggravate patellar tracking on a hard floor. The standing version (foot on chair/bed) avoids this entirely.
>
> **Pigeon stretch**
> Front shin roughly parallel to the front of the mat — or whatever angle allows the hip to relax without knee torque. Back leg extended behind, hips square. Sink the front hip toward the floor. For a deeper stretch, fold the torso forward over the front leg. Left side first, 60 sec each.
>
> Left-side standard: the left hip does not need to match the right in depth. What matters is whether the left hip feels smoother by the final 20 seconds of the hold. Start with the shin at whatever angle allows the hip to drop without the left knee feeling torqued. If the knee feels anything other than neutral pressure — even mild medial discomfort — pull the heel closer to the body and reduce the angle.
>
> Left hip ER stiffness is on the closing list. The trip is the window to confirm that trend. Log the left hip ER score after the pigeon (not before — we care whether the protocol moves it, not just what it starts at).
>
> **90/90 hip rotation**
> Sit on the floor, both legs at 90° (front leg externally rotated, back leg internally rotated). Rotate the pelvis to switch sides by tipping the back knee forward. Tall chest, hands light on the floor for balance only. 10 slow, controlled reps each side.
>
> The left ER phase (front leg external rotation) is the part to watch — slightly sticky is expected and acceptable. Clicking without pain is acceptable. Any pinching sensation in the left hip or knee means shorten the range and slow down further.
>
> Why this is here alongside the pigeon: pigeon stretches the hip in a fixed position; the 90/90 rotation moves through the range actively. Both are needed — passive range without active range doesn't transfer to the receiving positions in cleans and snatches.

**Phase 3 — Thoracic + Shoulder (4 min)**

- [ ] **Banded overhead distraction — 30 sec right arm, 40 sec left arm** (use 10–15 lb loop band)
- [ ] **Banded shoulder dislocates — 10 reps at 3-sec pace each direction** (use 5–10 lb loop band, widest comfortable grip)
- [ ] **Banded face pulls — 15 reps** (use 15–20 lb loop band anchored at face height)

> [!info]- Coaching — Thoracic + Shoulder
>
> **Banded overhead distraction**
> Anchor the loop band overhead — a door frame works: open the door, loop the band around the top of the door at the hinge side, close the door to trap it. Grip the band with one hand, thumb pointing up (supinated grip), step back until the band is taut and pulling the arm forward and overhead. Allow the arm to reach full overhead flexion. Keep the ribcage down — don't let the lower back arch to find extra range. Right arm: 30 sec. Left arm: 40 sec.
>
> The left shoulder is the known more-restricted side — the extra 10 seconds reflects that. The left arm will feel more blocked and less stretchy than the right. That's the known bilateral-worse-on-left thoracic restriction. Stay patient and breathe into the end range. Don't crank the band angle steeper to force more range — that loads the anterior shoulder, which is already on the watch list.
>
> **Why a loop band works here:** the loop band at the top of a door creates the same distraction as a pull-up band — the band tension provides a constant pull into the overhead position. The grip position (thumb up) is important: supination opens the shoulder joint and allows more true overhead range. If you grip with the thumb down (pronated), the humerus internally rotates and the range will feel blocked.
>
> Carryover target: after 6 days of daily overhead distraction, the May 6 OHS warm-up and front rack position should feel meaningfully less "left arm stuck." This is the primary thoracic mobility intervention available without a barbell.
>
> **Banded shoulder dislocates**
> Stand holding the loop band at its full width — or as wide as needed to move without pain. Slowly pass the band overhead and behind, then return. 3-second pace each direction: count "one-one-thousand, two-one-thousand, three-one-thousand" in each direction. 10 reps.
>
> A loop band works fine for this — hold it at its full circumference, stretched wide between both hands. The key cue is **ribs down the whole time**. If the band only clears overhead by arching the lower back, the grip is too narrow. Widen the grip until you can complete the movement with a flat or slightly tucked lumbar. The point is shoulder and thoracic movement, not demonstrating grip-width flexibility.
>
> Stop signals: anterior shoulder pinch, numbness anywhere in the arm, or a sharp sensation at the biceps tendon insertion. If any of these appear, skip the dislocates that day and add 5 extra face pulls instead.
>
> **Banded face pulls**
> Anchor the 15–20 lb loop band at face height (door handle, wrapped around a sturdy fixture). Hold both sides of the loop or loop it over both wrists. Pull toward your face with elbows high and wide — the finish position is hands by the ears, elbows spread wide, shoulder blades back and down. Pause 1 second at the end position. 15 reps.
>
> Target muscles: rear delts, mid-traps, lower traps. If you feel the neck and upper traps instead, lower the anchor point or reduce band tension. The pull path should be slightly upward — not horizontal — to clear the posterior shoulder under the acromion.
>
> Why this is here on a trip: face pulls are the primary posterior shoulder maintenance move available without a cable machine. They directly support jerk lockout stability — the mid and lower traps have to be active to hold the bar overhead. 6 days of daily face pulls means you'll return to May 6 with the rear shoulder ready, not deactivated.

**Phase 4 — Decompression (3 min)**

- [ ] **Cat-cow — 10 reps, 3 sec each direction**
- [ ] **Standing forward fold — 60 sec**

> [!info]- Coaching — Decompression
>
> **Cat-cow**
> On hands and knees. Cow: drop the belly, lift the head and tailbone, look up — lumbar extension. Cat: round the back, tuck the chin, tuck the tailbone — lumbar flexion. Move slowly — 3 seconds into each position — with one full breath per position. The goal is segmental motion: trying to move one vertebra at a time from the top of the spine to the bottom, rather than just hinging in two places.
>
> Right erector check: by rep 8–10, the right side should feel like it's moving more freely than at rep 1. If it feels like it's tightening or guarding as you move through range, shorten the range in both directions and slow down further. Don't push through guarding.
>
> **Standing forward fold**
> Feet hip-width, soft knees, slowly hinge forward and hang. Head heavy, arms hang loose. 60 seconds of just hanging and breathing. Let the spine decompress passively after the mobility work and after however many hours of travel sitting today.
>
> If you feel a sharp, localized sensation in the lumbar (not a broad stretch — a specific point that feels angry), come out of it and finish with child's pose instead. Broad hamstring and low-back stretch is normal. Specific lumbar point tenderness is a signal to back off.

### Corrective Block (~15 min)

- [ ] **Bulgarian split squat — 3 × 8 each leg, 3-sec eccentric, 4 kg DB in each hand, yoga block under rear foot** (rest 60 sec between legs)
- [ ] **Single-leg RDL — 2 × 8 each leg, 4 kg DB ipsilateral (same hand as standing leg)**
- [ ] **Banded clamshell — 2 × 15 each side, 5–10 lb loop band**

> [!info]- Coaching — Corrective Block (Workout A)
>
> **Bulgarian split squat**
> Setup: stand about 2 feet in front of a chair or bed, facing away. Place the top of the rear foot on the surface — yoga block under the rear foot gives consistent elevation if the surface is high. Hold 4 kg DBs in each hand at your sides. Descend by bending the front knee until the back knee nearly touches the floor. Drive back up through the front heel. 3-second eccentric: count "one-one-thousand, two-one-thousand, three-one-thousand" on the way down, normal speed back up. Left leg first each set.
>
> The 3-second eccentric is the entire corrective mechanism here — it loads the quad eccentrically through the same range that patellar tracking lives in, builds the tendon tolerance the left knee needs, and exposes any left-right asymmetry that faster reps would hide. 4 kg is the right load for tempo work at this stage; this is not a strength session.
>
> Left knee standard: 5/5 expected. If it drops to 4/5 (mild awareness, not pain), that's acceptable — note it. If it drops to 3/5 or you feel any patellar sensation, drop to bodyweight only. Front foot slight toe-out (15–20°) reduces patellar compression if needed.
>
> This is the scheduled Wk 5 corrective that was deferred from Wed Apr 29 (the session where 17 kg BSS was also deferred). 4 kg is appropriate — the 17 kg retest is scheduled for May 8.
>
> **Single-leg RDL**
> Stand on one leg, hold a 4 kg DB in the same-side hand (ipsilateral loading — right hand when standing on right leg). Hinge at the hip, keeping the back flat, reaching the DB toward the floor while the free leg extends behind you. Feel a deep hamstring stretch on the standing leg. Drive hips forward to stand. Slow and controlled — this is a hip hinge quality drill, not a balance test.
>
> This movement is here specifically to monitor the new right hip finding from Wk 5 Wed (deep sharp pain in the right hip during 3-sec concentric DL at 150 kg — first occurrence). The hypothesis is femoroacetabular loading under sustained hip-flexion angle at heavy load. At 4 kg, the load is negligible — if the deep right hip sensation appears here, even as a dull ache, that's important data pointing toward a structural sensitivity rather than a pure load issue. Log any sensation specifically: which hip, what quality (sharp/dull/ache), at what point in the movement (top/bottom/transition).
>
> If right hip sensation appears: stop the movement, log it, skip on subsequent days. This is not a "push through it" situation.
>
> **Banded clamshell**
> Lie on your side, 5–10 lb loop band just above the knees. Hips at about 45° of flexion, knees bent at 90°. Keeping the feet stacked, rotate the top knee upward as far as you can without the pelvis rolling back. Hold 1 second at the top, control the return. Left side gets extra attention — this is the direct left hip ER corrective.
>
> Drive the movement with the glute, not by rotating the pelvis or leaning the torso backward. If you feel the movement mostly in the hip flexors and TFL (front of hip), you're compensating. Reset: roll slightly forward, squeeze the glute before initiating the rep. 15 controlled reps beats 20 sloppy reps where the pelvis is doing the work.

### Accessory Block (~10 min)

- [ ] **Push-ups — 3 × 8 strict, 2-sec eccentric**
- [ ] **Dead bugs — 2 × 10 reps (alternating opposite arm + leg)**

> [!info]- Coaching — Accessory Block (Workout A)
>
> **Push-ups**
> Hands slightly wider than shoulder-width. 2-second eccentric: lower slowly, chest to floor, brief pause, full lockout at the top. Elbows track back at about 45° from the torso — not flared wide (shoulder impingement risk) and not tucked fully (unnecessary restriction). 3 sets of 8 strict reps with the 2-sec descent is meaningful pressing volume with bodyweight.
>
> Why push-ups and not something more exotic: you have no horizontal pressing equipment on the trip. Push-ups maintain the pressing pattern and, critically, the serratus anterior and anterior shoulder stability that supports jerk lockout. 6 days of no pressing at all means May 6 overhead work will feel slightly less stable at the top.
>
> Stop signal: if the anterior shoulder sensation that appeared at 105 kg clean on Wed (mild, 4.5/5) shows up during push-ups, stop immediately and skip the remaining sets. Log it. Push-ups should be a neutral movement for that limiter — if they're not, that's new information.
>
> **Dead bugs**
> Lie on your back, arms straight up toward the ceiling, hips and knees at 90° (tabletop legs). Lower opposite arm and leg simultaneously toward the floor — arm overhead, leg extended out — while keeping the lower back pressed against the floor. The lower back contact with the floor is the only cue that matters: if it lifts even slightly, you've gone too far. Return and switch sides. 10 reps = 5 each side.
>
> This is anti-extension core work — the core's job is to resist the lumbar extending under load, which is exactly what it needs to do for the right erector to stop overworking. When the deep core (transversus abdominis, diaphragm, pelvic floor) handles spinal stabilization through the dead bug range, the right erector doesn't need to grip constantly. This is the same mechanism as the 90/90 breathing, just made active.

### Corrective Block (~8 min)

- [ ] **Bulgarian split squat — 2 × 6 each leg, bodyweight, 3-sec eccentric, yoga block under rear foot**
- [ ] **Banded standing hip ER — 2 × 12 each side, 5–10 lb loop band**

> [!info]- Coaching — Corrective Block (Workout B)
>
> **Bulgarian split squat**
> Same setup as Workout A: yoga block under rear foot, left leg first, 3-sec eccentric. Bodyweight only. Two sets of 6 instead of three sets of 8. The load is lighter but the 3-second eccentric is still the corrective stimulus — the eccentric phase trains the quad and patellar tendon regardless of external load. After Workout A's 4 kg sets, bodyweight will feel noticeably easy; that's fine. The movement pattern and tempo are what matter.
>
> Left knee standard unchanged: 5/5 expected, anything below 4 means drop to a shallower range of motion rather than stopping entirely.
>
> **Banded standing hip ER**
> Stand on one leg. Loop the 5–10 lb band around the opposite ankle and anchor the other end to something low and fixed (door hinge at floor level, table leg). Drive the free leg into external rotation against the band — imagine trying to rotate the foot outward and backward. Keep the standing pelvis level: don't hike the hip on the banded side to create range. Hold 1 second at end range, control the return. 12 reps each side.
>
> This directly isolates the left hip ER pattern that the pigeon and 90/90 rotation address passively. The standing position means the glute medius on the standing leg is also working — beneficial for the left-right hip asymmetry that's been a thread through Phase 1. On the left side, you may feel it's harder to drive into full ER range than on the right — that's the known stiffness. Work within the available range without rotating the pelvis.

### Accessory Block (~6 min)

- [ ] **Dead bugs — 3 × 10 reps**
- [ ] **Ab roller — 2 × 5 reps from knees** *(only if right erector baseline today is 4+; skip if 3 or below)*

> [!info]- Coaching — Accessory Block (Workout B)
>
> **Dead bugs**
> Same as Workout A. Three sets today instead of two since it's the only mandatory accessory. Lower back on the floor the entire time — shorten the range of motion rather than letting the back lift.
>
> **Ab roller**
> From the knees only. Kneel on a yoga block or folded towel if the floor is hard. Grip the ab roller, start with arms straight, core braced. Roll forward slowly until the lower back is about to extend (you'll feel the lumbar starting to sag) — that's your stopping point. Pull back by contracting the abs. 5 reps.
>
> The gate is the right erector baseline score: if it's 4+ today, the deep stabilizers are in a good state and the ab roller will add useful anti-extension challenge. If it's 3 or below, the erector is already managing something and the ab roller will just load it further — skip it and do an extra set of dead bugs instead.
>
> On Day 6 (the final day before returning to lifting), skip the ab roller regardless of score. The goal on Day 6 is to arrive ready, not to squeeze in one last challenging set.

## Red-Flag Boundaries

- **Right erector pain worsening across the trip:** stop the foam roll compression, switch to light cross-friction only; keep breathing + couch stretch + cat-cow
- **Right hip sensation at SL-RDL (even dull ache):** stop the movement that session, log it precisely, skip SL-RDL on subsequent days
- **Left knee below 4/5 during BSS:** drop to bodyweight and reduce range; if still below 4, skip BSS that day
- **Anterior shoulder pinch during banded dislocates or overhead distraction:** reduce band tension and angle immediately; if it persists, skip those two movements and add 5 extra face pulls
- **Sharp / electrical / referring pain in the lumbar:** stop all loading, log, see a clinician on return if it persists across 2+ days
